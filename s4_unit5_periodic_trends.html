5.1

History of the Periodic Table

Döbereiner (1829): noted triads of elements with similar properties. Newlands (1864): law of octaves — every 8th element similar. Mendeleev (1869): arranged 63 elements by atomic mass, grouped by properties, left gaps for undiscovered elements (predicted Ga, Sc, Ge). Moseley (1913): showed atomic number (not mass) is the basis of the periodic law. Modern periodic table: elements arranged in order of increasing atomic number.

5.3

Atomic Radius Trends

Definition

Atomic radius = half the distance between nuclei of two bonded atoms of the same element.

DirectionTrendReason
Down a groupIncreasesMore electron shells added → outer electrons further from nucleus
Across a period (left to right)DecreasesNuclear charge increases → same shell electrons pulled closer; shielding nearly constant
5.4

Ionisation Energy Trends

DirectionTrend in IE₁Reason
Down a groupDecreasesMore shells → increased shielding → weaker attraction on outer electron
Across a periodGenerally increasesNuclear charge increases → stronger pull on outer electrons
Two anomalies across Period 2

Be → B: IE₁ decreases. B removes from 2p (higher energy than 2s). N → O: IE₁ decreases. N has stable half-filled 2p³; O has paired electron in 2p that is easier to remove.

5.5

Electronegativity Trends

Definition (Pauling scale)

Electronegativity measures the power of an atom to attract the shared electron pair in a covalent bond. F = 4.0 (highest).

DirectionTrendReason
Down a groupDecreasesIncreasing atomic radius and shielding → nucleus attracts bonding electrons less strongly
Across a periodIncreasesIncreasing nuclear charge; similar shielding → stronger attraction for bonding electrons
5.6

Melting Point Trends

Structure typeMelting pointExample across Period 3
Giant metallic (Na, Mg, Al)Moderate to high — increases with charge/electronsNa 98°C → Mg 650°C → Al 660°C
Giant covalent (Si)Very high — many covalent bondsSi 1414°C
Simple molecular (P₄, S₈, Cl₂, Ar)Low — only IMFs brokenS₈ 119°C, Cl₂ −101°C, Ar −189°C

The melting point trend across Period 3: rises from Na to Si (giant structures), then drops sharply at phosphorus (simple molecular P₄), continues to fall: S₈ > Cl₂ > Ar.
