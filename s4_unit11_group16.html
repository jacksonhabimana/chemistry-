Oxygen and Ozone

Oxygen occurs as O‚āā ‚ÄĒ a colourless, odourless gas. It makes up 21% of the atmosphere. It is paramagnetic (two unpaired electrons in its molecular orbitals ‚ÄĒ best explained by MO theory). O‚āā is the most abundant element on Earth's crust (by mass) when combined with other elements.

Industrial production: Fractional distillation of liquid air. Oxygen condenses at ‚ąí183¬įC; nitrogen at ‚ąí196¬įC ‚ÄĒ separated by fractional distillation. Oxygen is used in steelmaking (basic oxygen furnace), medical applications, and combustion.

Ozone (O‚āÉ) ‚ÄĒ the Allotrope of Oxygen

Ozone is the second allotrope of oxygen. Structure: bent/angular molecule, bond angle = 117¬į, bond order = 1.5 (resonance between two structures). O‚āÉ is formed from O‚āā by UV radiation in the upper atmosphere:

O‚āā + UV ‚Üí 2O‚ÄĘ (homolytic fission ‚ÄĒ radicals) O‚ÄĘ + O‚āā ‚Üí O‚āÉ (ozone formation) O‚āÉ + UV ‚Üí O‚āā + O‚ÄĘ (ozone destruction ‚ÄĒ equilibrium maintained) Net: stratospheric ozone absorbs UV-B and UV-C radiation, protecting life on Earth.

  • Much stronger oxidising agent than O‚āā ‚ÄĒ used to sterilise water and bleach
  • Decomposes to O‚āā: 2O‚āÉ ‚Üí 3O‚āā
  • Reacts with alkenes via ozonolysis: C=C + O‚āÉ ‚Üí carbonyl compounds

  • Ozone layer depletion: CFCs release Cl‚ÄĘ radicals in the stratosphere ‚Üí Cl‚ÄĘ + O‚āÉ ‚Üí ClO‚ÄĘ + O‚āā; ClO‚ÄĘ + O‚ÄĘ ‚Üí Cl‚ÄĘ + O‚āā (catalytic cycle ‚ÄĒ each Cl‚ÄĘ destroys thousands of O‚āÉ molecules)

Concentrated sulfuric acid (98%) is a colourless, oily, very dense liquid (density 1.84 g cm‚ĀĽ¬≥). It is miscible with water in all proportions.
