4.1

Covalent Bonding

A covalent bond forms when two atoms share a pair of electrons. Each atom contributes one electron (or both from one atom — dative bond). The shared electrons are attracted to both nuclei, holding the atoms together.

Bond types by number of shared pairs
BondShared pairsExampleBond strengthBond length
Single (σ)1H–H, C–HWeakestLongest
Double (σ+π)2C=C, C=OMediumMedium
Triple (σ+2π)3N≡N, C≡CStrongestShortest
Dative (Coordinate) Bond

Both electrons in the shared pair come from the same atom (the donor). Examples: NH₃→BF₃ adduct, H₃O⁺, NH₄⁺. Represented by an arrow → from donor to acceptor.

4.2

VSEPR Theory

Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion

Electron pairs (bonding and lone pairs) around a central atom repel each other and arrange themselves to be as far apart as possible. Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs.

4.3

Bond Polarity and Electronegativity

Electronegativity

Electronegativity is the ability of an atom in a covalent bond to attract the shared electron pair towards itself. Increases across a period and up a group (F is most electronegative).

When atoms of different electronegativity share electrons, the bond is polar — the electron density is unequal, creating partial charges (δ+ and δ−). If molecular geometry is asymmetric, the molecule has a net dipole moment and is polar overall.

4.4

Hybridisation

Atomic orbitals mix to form new hybrid orbitals of equal energy, used to form σ bonds. π bonds are formed by sideways overlap of unhybridised p orbitals.

Hydrogen Bonding Requirements

A hydrogen bond forms between: a hydrogen atom bonded to a highly electronegative atom (N, O, or F) — the H-bond donor — and a lone pair on another N, O, or F atom — the H-bond acceptor. Strength ≈ 20 kJ mol⁻¹ (much weaker than covalent bonds ≈ 400 kJ mol⁻¹, but stronger than London forces).
