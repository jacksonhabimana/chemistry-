Discovery of Subatomic Particles

The modern atom was revealed through landmark experiments. Dalton (1803) proposed indivisible atoms; later experiments proved atoms have internal structure.

Gold foil experiment: most α-particles passed straight through, but a few deflected at large angles, some bounced back. Conclusion: the atom is mostly empty space with a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus.

1.2

Atomic Number and Mass Number

Key Definitions

Atomic number (Z) = number of protons in the nucleus. Defines the element.
Mass number (A) = number of protons + neutrons (nucleons).
Number of neutrons = A − Z

Notation: ᴬ_Z X   e.g. ²³_₁₁Na means A=23, Z=11, so 11 protons, 12 neutrons, 11 electrons

In a neutral atom: number of electrons = number of protons = Z.

In ions: cations have lost electrons (fewer e⁻ than protons); anions have gained electrons (more e⁻ than protons).

Isotopes

Definition

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same Z) with different mass numbers (different number of neutrons).

ElementIsotopesProtonsNeutronsNatural abundance
Hydrogen¹H (protium), ²H (deuterium), ³H (tritium)10, 1, 299.98%, 0.02%, trace
Carbon¹²C, ¹³C, ¹⁴C66, 7, 898.9%, 1.1%, trace
Chlorine³⁵Cl, ³⁷Cl1718, 2075%, 25%
Uranium²³⁵U, ²³⁸U92143, 1460.72%, 99.27%

Isotopes have identical chemical properties (same electron configuration) but different physical properties (different mass → different density, boiling point, diffusion rate).

1.4

Relative Atomic Mass

Definition

Relative atomic mass (Aᵣ) is the weighted mean mass of atoms of an element compared to 1/12 the mass of a ¹²C atom.

Aᵣ = Σ (isotope mass × fractional abundance)
Worked Example — Chlorine

³⁵Cl: mass = 35, abundance = 75.77%  |  ³⁷Cl: mass = 37, abundance = 24.23%

1
Aᵣ = (35 × 0.7577) + (37 × 0.2423)
2
= 26.52 + 8.97 = 35.49
Worked Example — Boron

¹⁰B: 19.9%  |  ¹¹B: 80.1%

1
Aᵣ = (10 × 0.199) + (11 × 0.801) = 1.99 + 8.81 = 10.80
1.5

The Mass Spectrometer

The mass spectrometer separates ions by their mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) and measures the relative abundance of each.

StageNameWhat happens
1VaporisationSample is vaporised into a gas
2IonisationElectrons fired at gas atoms → knock out electrons → positive ions formed (M → M⁺ + e⁻)
3AccelerationPositive ions accelerated by electric field to high speed
4DeflectionIons deflected by magnetic field — lighter ions deflect more; heavier ions deflect less
5DetectionIons hit detector, produce current proportional to abundance
Key Point

Only positive ions are detected. The magnetic field separates ions of different m/z values. A graph of relative abundance vs m/z gives the mass spectrum.

1.6

Interpreting Mass Spectra

Each peak in a mass spectrum corresponds to an isotope. The x-axis shows m/z (≈ mass number for singly charged ions) and the y-axis shows relative abundance (%).

Worked Example — Calculate Aᵣ from Mass Spectrum of Neon

Peaks: m/z = 20 (abundance 90.9%), m/z = 21 (0.3%), m/z = 22 (8.8%)

1
Aᵣ = (20×90.9 + 21×0.3 + 22×8.8) / (90.9+0.3+8.8)
2
= (1818 + 6.3 + 193.6) / 100 = 2017.9/100 = 20.18

For diatomic molecules like Cl₂, the mass spectrum shows peaks for all isotope combinations: ³⁵Cl³⁵Cl (m/z=70), ³⁵Cl³⁷Cl (m/z=72), ³⁷Cl³⁷Cl (m/z=74) in ratio 9:6:1.

QUIZ

25-Question Quiz

Questions — Click "Show" for each answer

Q1

State the charge and mass of a proton, neutron, and electron.

Proton: charge +1, mass 1 u. Neutron: charge 0, mass 1 u. Electron: charge −1, mass ≈ 0 (1/1840 u).
Q2

Define atomic number and mass number.

Atomic number (Z) = number of protons. Mass number (A) = number of protons + neutrons.
Q3

How many protons, neutrons, and electrons does ⁵⁶Fe have?

Z=26 → 26 protons, 26 electrons. Neutrons = 56−26 = 30.
Q4

Define isotopes. Give one example with two isotopes.

Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. Example: ¹²C and ¹³C both have 6 protons but 6 and 7 neutrons respectively.
Q5

Why do isotopes have the same chemical properties?

Because chemical properties depend on the number and arrangement of electrons, which is the same for all isotopes of an element.
Q6

Define relative atomic mass.

The weighted mean mass of atoms of an element compared to 1/12 the mass of one atom of carbon-12.
Q7

Bromine has two isotopes: ⁷⁹Br (50.5%) and ⁸¹Br (49.5%). Calculate Aᵣ.

Aᵣ = (79×0.505) + (81×0.495) = 39.895 + 40.095 = 79.99 ≈ 80.0
Q8

State the five stages of the mass spectrometer in order.

1. Vaporisation 2. Ionisation 3. Acceleration 4. Deflection (by magnetic field) 5. Detection
Q9

Why must the inside of a mass spectrometer be kept at very low pressure (vacuum)?

To prevent ions colliding with air molecules, which would deflect them and give inaccurate results.
Q10

In a mass spectrometer, which ions are deflected more by the magnetic field — heavier or lighter ions? Explain.

Lighter ions are deflected more because the magnetic force causes greater change in direction for particles of smaller mass travelling at the same speed.
Q11

What does the x-axis of a mass spectrum represent?

Mass-to-charge ratio (m/z). For singly charged ions (z=1), this equals the mass number of the isotope.
Q12

What does the y-axis of a mass spectrum represent?

Relative abundance (or relative intensity) — the proportion of each isotope present.
Q13

Silicon has three isotopes: ²⁸Si (92.2%), ²⁹Si (4.7%), ³⁰Si (3.1%). Calculate Aᵣ.

Aᵣ = (28×0.922)+(29×0.047)+(30×0.031) = 25.816+1.363+0.930 = 28.11
Q14

State one difference between protons and neutrons.

Protons have a charge of +1; neutrons have no charge (charge = 0). Both have mass ≈ 1 u.
Q15

An ion ²⁷Al³⁺ has how many electrons?

Al has Z=13 (13 protons). Al³⁺ has lost 3 electrons → 13−3 = 10 electrons.
Q16

What experiment did Rutherford perform and what did he conclude?

Gold foil experiment — fired alpha particles at thin gold foil. Most passed through; some deflected at large angles. Conclusion: atoms are mostly empty space with a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus.
Q17

What was the plum pudding model and who proposed it?

J.J. Thomson proposed the plum pudding model: electrons (plums) embedded in a diffuse positive charge (pudding). Disproved by Rutherford's gold foil experiment.
Q18

How is ionisation achieved in a mass spectrometer?

Electron bombardment (EI) — high-energy electrons are fired at the vaporised sample, knocking out electrons to form positive ions: M + e⁻(fast) → M⁺ + 2e⁻
Q19

Chlorine-35 makes up 75% and Chlorine-37 makes up 25% of natural chlorine. What is Aᵣ?

Aᵣ = (35×0.75)+(37×0.25) = 26.25+9.25 = 35.50
Q20

What is the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) of a ⁴⁰Ca²⁺ ion?

m/z = 40/2 = 20
Q21

Why are isotopes useful in medicine? Give one example.

Radioactive isotopes can be used as tracers or in cancer treatment. Example: ¹³¹I (iodine-131) is used to treat thyroid cancer as the thyroid absorbs iodine.
Q22

Carbon-14 is used in radiocarbon dating. It has Z=6 and A=14. How many neutrons does it have?

Neutrons = A − Z = 14 − 6 = 8 neutrons.
Q23

The mass spectrum of an element shows peaks at m/z = 63 (69%) and m/z = 65 (31%). Calculate Aᵣ and identify the element.

Aᵣ = (63×0.69)+(65×0.31) = 43.47+20.15 = 63.62 ≈ 63.6. The element is Copper (Cu, Z=29).
Q24

Give two physical properties that differ between isotopes.

Any two of: density, boiling point, melting point, rate of diffusion, nuclear stability (radioactivity).
Q25

In the mass spectrum of Cl₂, three peaks appear at m/z = 70, 72, 74. Explain what causes each peak.

m/z=70: ³⁵Cl–³⁵Cl (35+35). m/z=72: ³⁵Cl–³⁷Cl (35+37). m/z=74: ³⁷Cl–³⁷Cl (37+37). The ratio of peak heights is 9:6:1.

[1] m/z=70 (³⁵Cl³⁵Cl): probability = 0.75×0.75 = 0.5625 [1] m/z=72 (³⁵Cl³⁷Cl): probability = 2×(0.75×0.25) = 0.375 [1] m/z=74 (³⁷Cl³⁷Cl): probability = 0.25×0.25 = 0.0625 [1] Ratio: 0.5625:0.375:0.0625 = 9:6:1 [1 for correct ratio]

Section C — Extended (10 marks)

C1 [5]

Describe how the mass spectrometer can be used to determine the Aᵣ of an element. Include how the instrument works and how you would use the data it produces.

[1] Sample vaporised and ionised by electron bombardment. [1] Ions accelerated by electric field and deflected by magnetic field — separated by m/z. [1] Detector records relative abundance of each m/z value. [1] Read m/z values (= isotope masses) and relative abundances from the mass spectrum. [1] Aᵣ = Σ(isotope mass × fractional abundance). Award marks for clarity and accuracy of description.
C2 [5]

Compare Thomson's plum pudding model with Rutherford's nuclear model. Explain how the gold foil experiment disproved the plum pudding model.

[1] Thomson: electrons embedded in diffuse positive charge spread over whole atom. [1] Rutherford: tiny dense positive nucleus surrounded by mostly empty space with electrons orbiting. [1] Plum pudding predicted all α-particles would pass through or deflect slightly (positive charge spread out). [1] Instead, some α-particles deflected at large angles and some bounced back — only possible if positive charge is concentrated in a tiny nucleus. [1] Therefore plum pudding was wrong and nuclear model proposed.
