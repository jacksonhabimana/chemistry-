Unit 2 Quiz — Alkanes

25 Questions · Select one answer each
Q1

What is the general molecular formula for alkanes?

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons with general formula CnH2n+2. Each carbon forms 4 bonds; end carbons have 3 H and middle carbons 2 H.
Q2

How many structural isomers does C4H10 have?

2 isomers: butane (straight chain) and 2-methylpropane (branched).
Q3

What process is used to separate petroleum into fractions?

Fractional distillation. Crude oil is heated; vapours rise up a fractionating column and condense at different levels based on their boiling points.
Q4

The IUPAC name of (CH3)3CH is:

(CH3)3CH = CH3CH(CH3)CH3. Longest chain = 3 C (propane), methyl at C2 &rarr; 2-methylpropane. &ldquo;Isobutane&rdquo; is only a common name.
Q5

Halogenation of alkanes requires:

Free-radical substitution requires UV light or high temperature to homolytically cleave Cl2 into radicals that initiate the chain reaction.
Q6

Which termination step produces ethane during chlorination of methane?

&bull;CH3 + &bull;CH3 &rarr; C2H6. Two methyl radicals combine &mdash; a minor termination step that produces ethane as a by-product.
Q7

Decarboxylation of sodium propanoate (CH3CH2COONa) with NaOH/CaO produces:

Sodium propanoate has 3 carbons. Decarboxylation removes &ndash;COO&minus;, giving a 2-carbon alkane: ethane.
Q8

Why are alkanes relatively unreactive with most chemical reagents?

C&ndash;C (347 kJ/mol) and C&ndash;H (413 kJ/mol) bonds are strong and non-polar. Non-polar bonds do not attract polar reagents (electrophiles or nucleophiles), so alkanes resist ionic attack.
Q9

Which petroleum fraction is used as jet fuel?

Kerosene (C10&ndash;C16, B.P. 150&ndash;270&deg;C) is used as aviation turbine fuel (jet fuel).
Q10

The purpose of catalytic cracking in petroleum refining is:

Catalytic cracking breaks large molecules into smaller, more useful ones including petrol-range alkanes (higher demand) and alkenes (feedstock for plastics). It uses a zeolite catalyst at 450&ndash;550&deg;C.
Q11

The reaction of methane with excess chlorine under UV light gives as final product:

With excess Cl₂ and UV, all 4 H atoms are progressively replaced: CH₄ → CH₃Cl → CH₂Cl₂ → CHCl₃ → CCl₄ (tetrachloromethane). CCl₄ is the final product.
Q12

Cyclohexane (C₆H₁₂) is a cycloalkane. Its general formula is:

Cycloalkanes: CₙH₂ₙ — same as alkenes but no double bond. The ring formation uses two H atoms less than the open chain. Cyclohexane (n=6): C₆H₁₂.
Q13

Which statement about the free-radical chain reaction of methane with Cl₂ is correct?

Termination: two radicals combine. e.g. 2Cl• → Cl₂; 2CH₃• → C₂H₆; CH₃• + Cl• → CH₃Cl. This removes chain carriers → ends the chain reaction.
Q14

Natural gas is a fossil fuel formed from:

Natural gas: formed from anaerobic decomposition of ancient marine organisms over millions of years. Non-renewable resource. Main component: methane (85-95%).
Q15

The reason alkanes are immiscible with water is:

'Like dissolves like': non-polar alkanes cannot form H-bonds with water. The energy cost of disrupting water's H-bond network outweighs weak alkane-water London interactions.
Q16

Reforming of naphtha (C₇-C₁₀ alkanes) using a platinum catalyst converts them to:

Catalytic reforming: straight-chain alkanes → aromatic rings (dehydrocyclisation) + H₂. Products: BTX (benzene, toluene, xylenes) with higher octane numbers. Pt catalyst.
Q17

The boiling point of hexane (C₆H₁₄) compared to pentane (C₅H₁₂) is:

Each additional –CH₂– unit adds surface area → stronger London forces → higher boiling point. Pentane: 36°C; hexane: 69°C. Pattern consistent throughout the alkane series.
Q18

Thermal cracking uses:

Thermal cracking: high T, no catalyst. C–C bonds break homolytically → radicals → smaller alkanes + alkenes + H₂.
Q19

The empirical formula of all alkanes with more than one carbon is:

For large alkanes (n>>1): CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ ≈ CₙH₂ₙ → empirical formula approaches CH₂. Actually for even n: C₄H₁₀ → empirical C₂H₅. More precisely: depends on specific compound.
Q20

Which alkane would be used as a component of diesel fuel?

Diesel: C₁₂-C₁₆ fraction (kerosene/gas oil range). Heavier than petrol (C₅-C₁₀) but lighter than fuel oil. Tetradecane is a typical diesel component.
Q21

When propane burns in insufficient oxygen, which products can form?

Incomplete combustion (limited O₂): C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O (complete); insufficient O₂ → CO and/or C produced. CO is toxic; soot is a particulate pollutant.
Q22

The structural isomer 2-methylbutane has a different boiling point from pentane because:

Same molecular formula C₅H₁₂ but different shapes. 2-methylbutane (branched) is more spherical → less intermolecular contact → weaker van der Waals → lower b.p.
Q23

The IUPAC name of CH₃CH₂CH(CH₃)CH₂CH₃ is:

Longest chain: 5 carbons (pentane). Methyl branch on C3 → 3-methylpentane. Number from end nearest branch: C1-C2-C3(CH₃)-C4-C5. Name: 3-methylpentane.
Q24

A mixture of hexane isomers in petrol is preferable to n-hexane because:

Octane rating measures resistance to pre-ignition (knocking). Branched alkanes: higher octane. 2,2,4-trimethylpentane (isooctane): octane rating = 100. n-heptane: 0. Branching is better.
Q25

Methane (CH₄) has a tetrahedral structure because:

4 bonding pairs, 0 lone pairs → tetrahedral. VSEPR: pairs repel equally → 109.5° H–C–H angles. Carbon is sp³ hybridised in all alkanes.
