Oxidising and Reducing Agents

Oxidising Agent A substance that oxidises another species ‚ÄĒ it causes oxidation by accepting electrons from the other species. The oxidising agent itself is reduced (gains electrons ‚Üí OS decreases).
Reducing Agent A substance that reduces another species ‚ÄĒ it causes reduction by donating electrons to the other species. The reducing agent itself is oxidised (loses electrons ‚Üí OS increases).
Example: Zn + Cu¬≤‚Āļ ‚Üí Zn¬≤‚Āļ + Cu Zn ‚Üí Zn¬≤‚Āļ + 2e‚ĀĽ (Zn is OXIDISED ‚ÄĒ loses e‚ĀĽ ‚ÄĒ Zn is the REDUCING AGENT) Cu¬≤‚Āļ + 2e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí Cu (Cu¬≤‚Āļ is REDUCED ‚ÄĒ gains e‚ĀĽ ‚ÄĒ Cu¬≤‚Āļ is the OXIDISING AGENT)

Rules for Assigning Oxidation Numbers

  1. Pure elements: OS = 0 (Na, Cl‚āā, O‚āā, Fe, etc.)
  2. Simple monoatomic ions: OS = charge of ion (Na‚Āļ = +1; Cl‚ĀĽ = ‚ąí1; Al¬≥‚Āļ = +3; S¬≤‚ĀĽ = ‚ąí2)

Electrochemical Cells

The Electrochemical Cell ‚ÄĒ Galvanic/Voltaic Cell

An electrochemical (galvanic) cell converts chemical energy to electrical energy using a spontaneous redox reaction. The two half-reactions are separated and connected by an external circuit and a salt bridge.

Cell notation: Zn(s) | Zn¬≤‚Āļ(aq) || Cu¬≤‚Āļ(aq) | Cu(s)

Convention: anode (oxidation) on the LEFT | salt bridge || cathode (reduction) on the RIGHT.

Salt bridge: Contains an electrolyte (KNO‚āÉ, KCl, NH‚āĄNO‚āÉ) in agar gel. Completes the circuit by allowing ion flow. Maintains electrical neutrality in each half-cell (K‚Āļ flows into cathode half-cell; NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ flows into anode half-cell).

Electrolysis

Principles of Electrolysis

Electrolysis uses electrical energy to drive a non-spontaneous redox reaction ‚ÄĒ the reverse of a galvanic cell.

Cathode (negative electrode) ‚ÄĒ reduction occurs: positive ions (cations) gain electrons here. Metal deposits or gases are produced at the cathode.
Anode (positive electrode) ‚ÄĒ oxidation occurs: negative ions (anions) lose electrons here. Gases (Cl‚āā, O‚āā) or dissolved metal is released at the anode.
ūüß†
ANODE vs CATHODE in electrolysis vs galvanic cells In BOTH: Anode = oxidation; Cathode = reduction. This never changes. What changes: in a galvanic cell, anode is negative (‚ąí) and cathode is positive (+). In electrolysis, anode is positive (+) and cathode is negative (‚ąí) ‚ÄĒ the external power supply forces current in the "wrong" direction.

Cathode (‚ąí): Na‚Āļ + e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí Na(l) (sodium metal deposited) Anode (+): 2Cl‚ĀĽ ‚Üí Cl‚āā(g) + 2e‚ĀĽ (chlorine gas produced) Overall: 2NaCl ‚Üí 2Na + Cl‚āā Industrial use: Downs cell (commercial production of Na metal and Cl‚āā)

Cathode: 2H‚Āļ + 2e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí H‚āā‚ÜĎ (H‚Āļ reduced) Anode: 2H‚āāO ‚Üí O‚āā‚ÜĎ + 4H‚Āļ + 4e‚ĀĽ (water oxidised ‚ÄĒ inert Pt anode) Ratio: H‚āā:O‚āā = 2:1 (by volume)

Electrolytic Refining of Copper

Anode (+): Cu(impure) ‚Üí Cu¬≤‚Āļ + 2e‚ĀĽ (impure copper dissolves) Cathode (‚ąí): Cu¬≤‚Āļ + 2e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí Cu(pure) (pure copper deposited) More reactive impurities (Zn, Fe, Ni) ‚Üí pass into solution but not deposited Less reactive impurities (Ag, Au, Pt) ‚Üí fall as "anode sludge" and are recovered

Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

Faraday's First Law: The mass of substance deposited/liberated at an electrode is proportional to the quantity of charge passed.

Faraday's Second Law: For the same charge, the masses of different substances deposited are proportional to their equivalent masses (M/n).

Reactivity Series and Displacement

The Electrochemical Series (Reactivity Series)

Metals listed in order of decreasing reactivity (increasing E¬į for M^n+/M reduction). The electrochemical series is derived from standard electrode potentials:

Most reactive ‚Üí K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Ni > Sn > Pb > H > Cu > Ag > Au ‚Üí Least reactive Increasing E¬į value (from K at ‚ąí2.93V to Au at +1.50V) More reactive metal = stronger reducing agent = more negative E¬į
