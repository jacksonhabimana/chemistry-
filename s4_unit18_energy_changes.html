18.1

Internal Energy and the First Law of Thermodynamics

Internal Energy (U)

The internal energy (U) of a system is the total energy stored within it — the sum of all kinetic energies (translational, rotational, vibrational) and potential energies (intermolecular, chemical bonds) of all particles in the system.

First Law of Thermodynamics Energy cannot be created or destroyed — it can only be transferred or converted from one form to another. The total energy of the universe is constant.

ΔU = q + w
where ΔU = change in internal energy, q = heat transferred to system, w = work done on system

In chemistry, most reactions occur at constant pressure (open to the atmosphere), so we use enthalpy (H) rather than internal energy.

18.2

Enthalpy and Enthalpy Change (ΔH)

Enthalpy (H)

Enthalpy is defined as: H = U + pV (internal energy + pressure × volume). For reactions at constant pressure, the heat exchanged equals the enthalpy change:

ΔH = H(products) − H(reactants) ΔH < 0 → exothermic (heat released to surroundings) ΔH > 0 → endothermic (heat absorbed from surroundings) Units: kJ mol⁻¹ (per mole of reaction as written)
🧠
Sign convention — always from the system's perspective Exothermic: system loses energy → ΔH is NEGATIVE (−). The surroundings get warmer.
Endothermic: system gains energy → ΔH is POSITIVE (+). The surroundings get cooler.
18.3

Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

Exothermic Reactions

Reactions that release heat to the surroundings. The temperature of the surroundings increases. Products have lower enthalpy than reactants.

Examples of exothermic reactions: • Combustion: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O ΔH = −890 kJ/mol • Neutralisation: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O ΔH = −57.1 kJ/mol • Respiration: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O ΔH = −2803 kJ/mol • Formation MgO: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO ΔH = −1204 kJ/mol • Oxidation Fe: 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃ ΔH = −1648 kJ/mol

Endothermic Reactions

Reactions that absorb heat from the surroundings. The temperature of the surroundings decreases. Products have higher enthalpy than reactants.

Examples of endothermic reactions: • Thermal decomp: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ ΔH = +178 kJ/mol • Photosynthesis: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ ΔH = +2803 kJ/mol • Dissolving NH₄NO₃: NH₄NO₃(s) → NH₄⁺(aq)+NO₃⁻(aq) ΔH = +25.7 kJ/mol • Evaporation: H₂O(l) → H₂O(g) ΔH = +44 kJ/mol
💡
Practical tests for exo/endothermic Exothermic: test tube feels HOT; thermometer rises. Endothermic: test tube feels COLD; thermometer drops. Dissolving ammonium nitrate in water is a classic endothermic demonstration — the beaker becomes ice-cold.
18.4

Standard Enthalpy Changes

Standard Conditions and Standard Enthalpy

Standard enthalpy changes (ΔH°) are measured under standard conditions: 298 K (25°C), 100 kPa pressure, all species in their standard states.

NameSymbolDefinitionExample
Standard Enthalpy of FormationΔH°fEnthalpy change when 1 mol of compound is formed from its elements in their standard statesC(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g) ΔH°f = −394 kJ/mol
Standard Enthalpy of CombustionΔH°cEnthalpy change when 1 mol of substance burns completely in excess oxygenCH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O ΔH°c = −890 kJ/mol
Standard Enthalpy of NeutralisationΔH°nEnthalpy change when 1 mol of water is formed in an acid-base neutralisationH⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O ΔH°n = −57.1 kJ/mol
Standard Enthalpy of AtomisationΔH°atEnthalpy change when 1 mol of gaseous atoms is formed from element in standard state½Cl₂(g) → Cl(g) ΔH°at = +121 kJ/mol
Standard Enthalpy of SolutionΔH°solEnthalpy change when 1 mol of substance dissolves in excess solventNaCl(s) → Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) ΔH°sol = +3.9 kJ/mol
💡
Key rule: ΔH°f of any element in standard state = 0 The enthalpy of formation of H₂(g), O₂(g), C(graphite), Na(s), Fe(s) etc. is ZERO by definition — they are already in their standard states, so no formation is needed.
18.7

Energy Profile Diagrams

Energy Profile for Exothermic Reactions

In an exothermic reaction, the products have lower enthalpy than the reactants. The curve rises to a peak (the transition state / activated complex) then falls to a lower level.

Energy Profile for Endothermic Reactions

In an endothermic reaction, products have higher enthalpy than reactants. The curve rises to a peak then falls to a level still above the starting point.

Effect of a Catalyst on the Energy Profile

A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. It does NOT change ΔH (same reactants and products → same enthalpy difference). On the diagram, the peak is lower but the start and end levels stay the same.

Without catalyst: Ea = large → slow reaction With catalyst: Ea = smaller → faster reaction ΔH: UNCHANGED by catalyst Kc: UNCHANGED by catalyst (only affects rate, not equilibrium)

Exercises

  • Define the following terms with one example and a thermochemical equation for each: (a) standard enthalpy of formation (b) standard enthalpy of combustion (c) exothermic reaction (d) endothermic reaction. Include the sign of ΔH for each example.
    ΔHf°: enthalpy change when 1 mol of compound forms from its elements in standard states at 298 K, 100 kPa. Example: C(s) + O2(g) → CO2(g), ΔHf° = −394 kJ/mol. ΔHc°: enthalpy change when 1 mol of substance burns completely in excess O2, standard conditions. Example: CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(l), ΔHc° = −890 kJ/mol. Exothermic: ΔH < 0; products lower energy than reactants; heat released. Example: combustion. Endothermic: ΔH > 0; products higher energy; heat absorbed. Example: photosynthesis.
  • Using the standard enthalpies of formation below, calculate ΔH° for each reaction: (a) 2SO2(g) + O2(g) → 2SO3(g) [ΔH°f: SO2 = −297, SO3 = −396, O2 = 0 kJ/mol] (b) C3H8(g) + 5O2(g) → 3CO2(g) + 4H2O(l) [ΔH°f: C3H8 = −104, CO2 = −394, H2O = −286 kJ/mol]
    (a) 2SO2 + O2 → 2SO3: ΔH° = ΣΔHf°(products) − ΣΔHf°(reactants) = 2(−396) − [2(−297) + 0] = −792 − (−594) = −198 kJ/mol. (b) C3H8 + 5O2 → 3CO2 + 4H2O: ΔH° = [3(−394) + 4(−286)] − [−104 + 0] = [−1182 − 1144] − (−104) = −2326 + 104 = −2222 kJ/mol.
  • Draw and fully label energy profile diagrams for: (a) an exothermic reaction (b) an endothermic reaction. Mark: reactants, products, transition state, Ea(forward), Ea(reverse), and ΔH. On diagram (a), show the effect of adding a catalyst.
    (a) Exothermic profile: Reactants at higher energy level → hump (transition state/peak) → Products at lower energy level. Label: Ea(forward) = reactants to peak; Ea(reverse) = products to peak (larger); ΔH = negative downward arrow from reactants to products. Catalyst: draw second lower peak; same reactant/product levels; Ea(cat) < Ea(uncat); ΔH unchanged. (b) Endothermic profile: Reactants at lower energy → hump → Products at higher energy. Label: Ea(forward) = larger; Ea(reverse) = smaller; ΔH = positive upward arrow.
  • State the First Law of Thermodynamics. Define internal energy (U) and enthalpy (H). Explain why chemists use enthalpy rather than internal energy to describe most reactions in the laboratory.
    First Law: energy cannot be created or destroyed; ΔU = q + w (heat + work). Internal energy U: total kinetic and potential energy of all particles in the system. Enthalpy H = U + PV; at constant pressure ΔH = qp (heat exchanged at constant P). Chemists use ΔH because lab reactions occur in open containers at constant atmospheric pressure; measuring ΔH directly gives the heat exchanged without needing to separately measure PV work. ΔU requires calorimetry in sealed bomb calorimeters to also account for Δ(PV).
  • The standard enthalpy of combustion of ethanol (C2H5OH) is −1371 kJ/mol. The standard enthalpies of formation of CO2(g) and H2O(l) are −394 and −286 kJ/mol respectively. Write the combustion equation and use these values to calculate ΔH°f for ethanol.
    Combustion of ethanol: C2H5OH(l) + 3O2(g) → 2CO2(g) + 3H2O(l), ΔHc° = −1371 kJ/mol. ΔHc° = [2(−394) + 3(−286)] − [ΔHf°(C2H5OH) + 0]. −1371 = [−788 − 858] − ΔHf°. −1371 = −1646 − ΔHf°. ΔHf°(C2H5OH) = −1646 + 1371 = −275 kJ/mol.
  • Explain the meaning of activation energy using an energy profile diagram. Why do some reactions with large negative ΔH proceed very slowly? Give one example. How does a catalyst help without violating the First Law of Thermodynamics?
    Activation energy Ea: minimum energy that colliding molecules must possess for a reaction to occur. On an energy profile: Ea = energy difference from reactants to the transition state peak. Importance: determines reaction rate — high Ea means fewer molecules have sufficient energy → slow reaction. A reaction with large negative ΔH can be very slow if Ea is large: e.g. diamond combustion (C + O2 → CO2, ΔH = −395 kJ/mol) — thermodynamically favourable but diamonds don't burn at room temperature because Ea is extremely high. Catalyst helps by providing an alternative pathway with lower Ea; more molecules succeed; does not violate First Law because ΔH (total energy change) is unchanged — only the pathway changes.

Multiple Choice Quiz — 25 Questions

Unit 18: Energy Changes

25 Questions
Q1

A reaction in which ΔH is NEGATIVE is described as:

ΔH < 0: the enthalpy of products is lower than reactants → energy released to surroundings → temperature of surroundings increases → exothermic. ΔH > 0: endothermic. Examples of exothermic: combustion, neutralisation, respiration.
Q2

The standard enthalpy of formation of any ELEMENT in its standard state is:

By definition, ΔH°f of any element in its standard state = 0. This is the reference point. Examples: O2(g), Fe(s), C(graphite), H2(g) — all have ΔH°f = 0. Diamond (C, allotrope) has ΔH°f = +2 kJ/mol (not 0, as graphite is the standard state).
Q3

What does the term "standard conditions" mean in thermochemistry?

Standard conditions: T = 298 K (25°C), P = 100 kPa (1 bar), concentration = 1 mol dm−3 for solutions. Not to be confused with STP (0°C, 1 atm) used for gas volumes.
Q4

In an energy profile diagram for an EXOTHERMIC reaction, the products are:

Exothermic: products have LESS energy (lower enthalpy) than reactants → ΔH is negative → energy released to surroundings. The energy profile shows reactants at higher level, products at lower, with a hump (transition state) in between representing activation energy.
Q5

Which of the following processes is ENDOTHERMIC?

Photosynthesis absorbs light energy (endothermic — ΔH > 0). Products (glucose, O2) have more enthalpy than reactants (CO2, H2O). Combustion, neutralisation, and rusting are all exothermic.
Q6

The standard enthalpy of formation of water is −286 kJ/mol. This means:

ΔH°f = −286 kJ/mol for H2O means: H2(g) + ½O2(g) → H2O(l), ΔH° = −286 kJ/mol. One mole of liquid water is formed from its elements under standard conditions with 286 kJ released.
Q7

What is the enthalpy change (ΔH°) for: CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(l), given ΔH°f: CH4 = −75, CO2 = −394, H2O(l) = −286, O2 = 0 kJ/mol?

ΔH°rxn = ΣΔH°f(products) − ΣΔH°f(reactants). Products: −394 + 2(−286) = −394 − 572 = −966. Reactants: −75 + 0 = −75. ΔH° = −966 − (−75) = −966 + 75 = −891 kJ/mol.
Q8

The activation energy (Ea) in an energy profile diagram is defined as:

Ea is the energy barrier that reactants must overcome to react. It is the energy difference between reactants and the transition state (peak). A higher Ea means fewer collisions have sufficient energy → slower reaction. Catalyst lowers Ea without changing ΔH.
Q9

How does a catalyst affect an energy profile diagram?

Catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy. The reactant and product energy levels (and hence ΔH) are UNCHANGED. Only the height of the energy barrier (Ea) decreases. Both forward and reverse Ea decrease by the same amount.
Q10

The standard enthalpy of combustion is defined as the enthalpy change when:

ΔH°c: one mole of the substance fully combusted in excess O2, all substances in standard states (products H2O as liquid, CO2 as gas). Always negative for organic compounds. Example: ΔH°c(ethanol) = −1371 kJ/mol.
Q11

For the reaction: C(s) + O2(g) → CO2(g), ΔH° = −394 kJ/mol. This is the standard enthalpy of:

This reaction is simultaneously: (1) the standard enthalpy of combustion of carbon (C burns in O2) and (2) the standard enthalpy of formation of CO2 (CO2 formed from its elements). Both definitions apply here.
Q12

In which energy profile diagram is the activation energy for the REVERSE reaction GREATER than for the forward reaction?

Exothermic reaction: products lower than reactants. Ea(forward) = energy from reactants to peak. Ea(reverse) = energy from products to peak — this is LARGER because products are at a lower level. Ea(reverse) = Ea(forward) + |ΔH|.
Q13

Which of the following reactions represents the standard enthalpy of formation of ethanol (C2H5OH)?

Standard enthalpy of formation: one mole of compound formed from its ELEMENTS in their standard states. For ethanol: 2C(s,graphite) + 3H2(g) + ½O2(g) → C2H5OH(l). Option B is the combustion of ethanol.
Q14

The sign convention for ΔH is based on:

Thermochemistry convention: ΔH is measured from the system's perspective. ΔH < 0 (exothermic): system loses energy (releases to surroundings). ΔH > 0 (endothermic): system gains energy (absorbs from surroundings).
Q15

For a reaction with a large activation energy, the reaction will be:

Large Ea: only a small fraction of collisions have sufficient energy → slow reaction rate. To increase rate: increase temperature (more particles have energy ≥ Ea) or use a catalyst (lowers Ea). Rate and thermodynamics (ΔH, spontaneity) are separate concepts.
Q16

Photosynthesis absorbs energy from sunlight. On an energy profile diagram, the reactants (CO2 + H2O) are:

Endothermic reaction: products have MORE energy than reactants. Reactants are at a LOWER energy level; products are at a HIGHER level. ΔH > 0. Energy is stored in the chemical bonds of glucose.
Q17

The enthalpy of NEUTRALISATION of a strong acid with a strong base is approximately −57 kJ/mol. This is constant because:

Strong acid + strong base: both fully dissociated. The only reaction occurring is H+ + OH− → H2O. This ionic equation is the same regardless of which strong acid/base is used → same ΔH = −57 kJ/mol. For weak acid/base, extra energy needed for dissociation → less exothermic.
Q18

The transition state in an energy profile diagram represents:

The transition state (activated complex) is the highest-energy arrangement of atoms during the reaction — bonds partially broken and formed simultaneously. It is not a stable intermediate; it cannot be isolated. It sits at the peak of the energy profile curve.
Q19

For the reaction N2(g) + 3H2(g) → 2NH3(g), ΔH° = −92 kJ/mol. The enthalpy of formation of NH3 is:

The equation given produces 2 moles of NH3. Standard enthalpy of formation is per ONE mole. ΔH°f(NH3) = −92 / 2 = −46 kJ/mol. The formation equation must be: ½N2(g) + 3/2H2(g) → NH3(g), ΔH°f = −46 kJ/mol.
Q20

Which of the following describes an ENDOTHERMIC energy profile?

Endothermic: products at HIGHER energy than reactants → ΔH > 0 → net energy absorbed from surroundings. There is still an activation energy hump (required for any reaction). Ea(forward) > Ea(reverse) for endothermic reactions.
Q21

The internal energy U of a system increases when:

First Law of Thermodynamics: ΔU = q + w. U increases when: (1) heat q > 0 (heat absorbed by system) OR (2) work w > 0 (work done ON the system). U decreases when heat is lost or system does work on surroundings.
Q22

What is the standard state of carbon for thermochemical purposes?

Graphite is the standard (most stable) state of carbon at 298 K and 100 kPa. ΔH°f(graphite) = 0 by definition. ΔH°f(diamond) = +2 kJ/mol because diamond is slightly less stable than graphite.
Q23

For the enthalpy level diagram of an exothermic reaction A → B (ΔH = −80 kJ/mol) with activation energy 150 kJ/mol, the activation energy for the REVERSE reaction B → A is:

Ea(reverse) = Ea(forward) + |ΔH| = 150 + 80 = 230 kJ/mol. The energy to go from B (products, lower) to the transition state (peak) is greater than from A (reactants, higher) to the peak by exactly the energy difference ΔH.
Q24

Which of the following is NOT a standard enthalpy of formation?

Standard enthalpy of formation must produce EXACTLY ONE MOLE of the compound. Option B produces 2 moles of H2O → this is 2 × ΔH°f(H2O), not ΔH°f itself. Options A, C, D all produce exactly 1 mole of product from elements.
Q25

On the energy profile diagram for a catalysed reaction, which quantities CHANGE compared to the uncatalysed reaction?

Catalyst: (1) provides alternative pathway with lower Ea. (2) Does NOT change ΔH (the energy difference between reactants and products is fixed by bond energies — unchanged). (3) Does NOT change the energy of reactants or products. Only the height of the energy barrier decreases.

Q1 [5 marks]

State and explain the First Law of Thermodynamics. Define internal energy (U) and enthalpy (H). Explain why enthalpy is more useful than internal energy for reactions studied in the laboratory at constant pressure. [5]

First Law: Energy cannot be created or destroyed — the total energy of the universe is constant. For a system: ΔU = q + w, where q = heat exchanged, w = work done. [1] Internal energy U: total kinetic and potential energy of all particles in the system. ΔU = q + w (heat + work). [1] Enthalpy H = U + PV. At constant pressure (laboratory): ΔH = ΔU + PΔV. Work term PΔV accounts for expansion against atmosphere. [1] Why enthalpy is useful: most lab reactions occur in open containers at constant P. ΔH equals the heat exchanged at constant P (qp = ΔH). ΔU would require measuring both q AND PΔV work — difficult experimentally. Enthalpy captures the heat change directly. [2]

Q7 [10 marks]

(a) Define the standard enthalpy of formation and the standard enthalpy of combustion. Explain how standard enthalpies of formation can be used to calculate the enthalpy change of any reaction using the formula ΔH°rxn = ΣΔH°f(products) − ΣΔH°f(reactants). [4] (b) Use the following data to calculate ΔH° for the reaction: 4NH3(g) + 5O2(g) → 4NO(g) + 6H2O(l). ΔH°f: NH3(g) = −46; NO(g) = +90; H2O(l) = −286; O2(g) = 0 kJ/mol. [3] (c) Explain why the standard enthalpy of formation of O2(g) is zero, but the standard enthalpy of formation of O3(g) (ozone) is +143 kJ/mol. [3]

ΔH°c: enthalpy change when ONE mole of substance burns completely in excess O2, standard conditions, giving CO2(g) and H2O(l). Formula justification: elements (ΔH°f = 0) can be thought of as a "common baseline". Any reaction can be expressed as: reactants → elements → products. Q8 [10 marks]

(a) Explain what activation energy is and why it is important for understanding reaction rates. Draw an energy profile for a reaction with Ea = 100 kJ/mol and ΔH = +40 kJ/mol, calculating Ea(reverse). [4] (b) Explain why a reaction with large negative ΔH might still be very slow at room temperature. Give one real-world example. [3] (c) Explain what the term "standard enthalpy of neutralisation" means and why it is approximately −57 kJ/mol for all strong acid + strong base reactions. Why is it less exothermic for weak acid + strong base? [3]

(a) Activation energy Ea: minimum energy that colliding particles must possess for a reaction to occur. It represents the energy needed to break bonds and reach the transition state. High Ea → fewer molecules have sufficient energy → slow rate. Low Ea (or catalyst) → more successful collisions → faster. [2] Diagram: endothermic (ΔH = +40). Reactants at 0 → peak at +100 (Ea = 100) → products at +40. Ea(reverse) = 100 − 40 = 60 kJ/mol. [2] (b) Diamond combustion: C(diamond) + O2 → CO2, ΔH = −395 kJ/mol (very exothermic) yet diamonds do not spontaneously burn at room temperature. This is because Ea is extremely high — bonds in diamond are very strong; very few molecules have sufficient energy to initiate the reaction at room temperature. Thermodynamic feasibility (large negative ΔH) does NOT guarantee kinetic speed. [3] (c) Standard enthalpy of neutralisation: ΔH when 1 mole of H2O is formed by reaction of acid and base in dilute solution, standard conditions. Strong acid + strong base: both fully dissociated; net ionic equation is always H+(aq) + OH−(aq) → H2O(l), ΔH = −57 kJ/mol. Same for any strong acid/base pair. Weak acid + strong base: weak acid is only partially dissociated; additional energy is needed to fully dissociate it (endothermic dissociation step). This partially offsets the exothermic neutralisation → less negative ΔH (e.g. −55 kJ/mol for CH3COOH + NaOH). [3]
