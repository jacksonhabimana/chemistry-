18.1

Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

Every chemical reaction involves energy change. When bonds break, energy is absorbed from the surroundings. When new bonds form, energy is released to the surroundings. The net energy change determines whether a reaction heats or cools its surroundings.

TypeHeat flowSign of ΔHTemperature changeExamples
ExothermicSystem → SurroundingsNegative (−)Surroundings get hotterCombustion, neutralisation, respiration, rusting
EndothermicSurroundings → SystemPositive (+)Surroundings get colderThermal decomposition, photosynthesis, dissolving NH₄NO₃, citric acid + NaHCO₃
Key Definition — Enthalpy Change (ΔH)

Enthalpy (H) is the heat content of a system at constant pressure. The enthalpy change (ΔH) is the heat energy transferred between system and surroundings during a reaction at constant pressure.

DefinitionΔH = Hproducts − Hreactants

Units: kJ mol⁻¹ (kilojoules per mole of reaction as written)

The First Law of Thermodynamics states that energy cannot be created or destroyed — only transferred or converted. This underpins all enthalpy calculations: energy released by a reaction equals energy absorbed by the surroundings.

Common Exothermic Reactions
ReactionΔH (approx.)
CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)−890 kJ mol⁻¹
H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l)−57.3 kJ mol⁻¹
C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)−394 kJ mol⁻¹
2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)−572 kJ mol⁻¹
18.2

Standard Enthalpy Changes

To allow fair comparison of energy data, chemists define standard conditions: temperature 298 K (25 °C), pressure 100 kPa, all substances in their standard states. The symbol ΔH° (or ΔH⊖) denotes a standard enthalpy change.

NameSymbolDefinitionExample equation
Standard enthalpy of formationΔH°f Enthalpy change when 1 mol of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states C(s) + 2H₂(g) → CH₄(g)   ΔH°f = −74.8 kJ mol⁻¹
Standard enthalpy of combustionΔH°c Enthalpy change when 1 mol of a substance is completely burned in excess oxygen under standard conditions C₂H₅OH(l) + 3O₂(g) → 2CO₂(g) + 3H₂O(l)   ΔH°c = −1367 kJ mol⁻¹
Standard enthalpy of neutralisationΔH°neut Enthalpy change when 1 mol of water is formed in an acid-base neutralisation reaction HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)   ΔH°neut = −57.3 kJ mol⁻¹
Standard enthalpy of atomisationΔH°at Enthalpy change when 1 mol of gaseous atoms is formed from the element in its standard state ½Cl₂(g) → Cl(g)   ΔH°at = +121 kJ mol⁻¹
Standard enthalpy of solutionΔH°sol Enthalpy change when 1 mol of a substance is completely dissolved in water to form an infinitely dilute solution NaCl(s) + aq → Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)   ΔH°sol = +3.9 kJ mol⁻¹
Lattice enthalpyΔH°latt Enthalpy change when 1 mol of an ionic compound is formed from its gaseous ions (exothermic, negative) Na⁺(g) + Cl⁻(g) → NaCl(s)   ΔH°latt = −787 kJ mol⁻¹
Electron affinityΔH°ea Enthalpy change when 1 mol of gaseous atoms each gains an electron to form anions Cl(g) + e⁻ → Cl⁻(g)   ΔH°ea = −349 kJ mol⁻¹
Ionisation energyΔH°IE Enthalpy change when 1 mol of electrons is removed from 1 mol of gaseous atoms (always endothermic) Na(g) → Na⁺(g) + e⁻   ΔH°IE = +496 kJ mol⁻¹
Common Error

ΔH°f of any element in its standard state is always zero by definition. For example: ΔH°f[O₂(g)] = 0, ΔH°f[C(graphite)] = 0, ΔH°f[Na(s)] = 0.

18.3

Energy Profile Diagrams

An energy profile diagram (or reaction profile) shows how the potential energy of reactants and products changes along the reaction pathway. The reaction coordinate on the x-axis represents progress from reactants to products, not time.

Activation Energy & Catalysts

The activation energy (Eₐ) is the minimum energy reactant particles must possess to react when they collide. It represents the energy needed to break bonds in reactants and reach the transition state (the highest-energy intermediate arrangement of atoms).

A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. This increases the proportion of particles with sufficient energy to react, speeding up the reaction. Importantly, a catalyst does not change ΔH — the start and end energy levels remain the same; only the peak is lowered.

18.4

Hess's Law and Enthalpy Cycles

Hess's Law (1840)

Hess's Law states that the total enthalpy change for a chemical reaction is the same regardless of the route taken, provided the initial and final conditions are the same.

This is a consequence of the First Law of Thermodynamics — enthalpy is a state function, so ΔH depends only on the initial and final states, not the pathway between them.

Using Hess's Law — Two Main Methods

Method 1: Using ΔH°f values

Standard formula ΔH°rxn = Σ ΔH°f(products) − Σ ΔH°f(reactants)
Worked Example 1 — Using ΔH°f

Find ΔH° for: CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(l)

Given: ΔH°f[CH₄(g)] = −74.8 kJ mol⁻¹, ΔH°f[CO₂(g)] = −393.5 kJ mol⁻¹, ΔH°f[H₂O(l)] = −285.8 kJ mol⁻¹, ΔH°f[O₂(g)] = 0

1
Sum ΔH°f of products: (−393.5) + 2×(−285.8) = −393.5 − 571.6 = −965.1 kJ mol⁻¹
2
Sum ΔH°f of reactants: (−74.8) + 2×(0) = −74.8 kJ mol⁻¹
3
ΔH°rxn = −965.1 − (−74.8) = −890.3 kJ mol⁻¹

Method 2: Using ΔH°c values (combustion enthalpy cycles)

Standard formula ΔH°rxn = Σ ΔH°c(reactants) − Σ ΔH°c(products)

Note: this formula is reversed compared to the formation formula. With combustion data, reactants come first.

Worked Example 2 — Using ΔH°c

Find ΔH°f for ethanol using: ΔH°c[C] = −394 kJ mol⁻¹, ΔH°c[H₂] = −286 kJ mol⁻¹, ΔH°c[C₂H₅OH] = −1367 kJ mol⁻¹

Equation for formation: 2C(s) + 3H₂(g) + ½O₂(g) → C₂H₅OH(l)

1
Sum ΔH°c of reactants: 2×(−394) + 3×(−286) = −788 − 858 = −1646 kJ mol⁻¹
2
Sum ΔH°c of products: 1×(−1367) = −1367 kJ mol⁻¹
3
ΔH°f[C₂H₅OH] = −1646 − (−1367) = −279 kJ mol⁻¹
Enthalpy Cycle Diagram (Hess's Law)

ΔH°f = ΔH°c(reactants) − ΔH°c(products). The green route down and blue route up must equal the yellow direct route.

18.5

Bond Enthalpies

Every covalent bond has a bond enthalpy (also called bond dissociation energy): the energy required to break one mole of that bond in gaseous molecules, averaged across different chemical environments.

Bond Breaking vs Bond Forming
  • Bond breaking → always endothermic (+) — energy must be put in
  • Bond forming → always exothermic (−) — energy is released
Overall ruleΔH°rxn = Σ Bond enthalpies (bonds broken) − Σ Bond enthalpies (bonds formed)

If more energy is released forming bonds than is used breaking them → exothermic (ΔH negative). If less → endothermic.

Worked Example — Bond Enthalpy Calculation for CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
1
Bonds broken (reactants): 4×(C–H) + 2×(O=O) = 4×413 + 2×498 = 1652 + 996 = +2648 kJ
2
Bonds formed (products): 2×(C=O) in CO₂ + 4×(O–H) in 2H₂O = 2×743 + 4×463 = 1486 + 1852 = 3338 kJ
3
ΔH° = 2648 − 3338 = −690 kJ mol⁻¹ (exothermic ✓)

Note: this differs from the experimental value (−890 kJ mol⁻¹) because bond enthalpies are averages. Bond enthalpy calculations give approximate ΔH values only.

18.6

Calorimetry

Calorimetry is the experimental method for measuring enthalpy changes. Heat energy transferred to or from a solution (or calorimeter) causes a temperature change, which can be measured with a thermometer.

Core Equation
q = m c ΔT
SymbolQuantityTypical units
qHeat energy transferredJoules (J)
mMass of solution (or water)grams (g)
cSpecific heat capacity of water4.18 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹
ΔTTemperature change (Tfinal − Tinitial)°C (or K)
Worked Example — Enthalpy of Neutralisation

25.0 cm³ of 1.00 mol dm⁻³ HCl is mixed with 25.0 cm³ of 1.00 mol dm⁻³ NaOH. Temperature rises from 22.0 °C to 28.7 °C.

1
Total volume = 50.0 cm³ → mass of solution ≈ 50.0 g (assuming density ≈ 1 g cm⁻³)
2
ΔT = 28.7 − 22.0 = 6.7 °C
3
q = 50.0 × 4.18 × 6.7 = 1400 J = 1.40 kJ
4
Moles of water formed = 0.025 × 1.00 = 0.025 mol
5
ΔHneut = −1.40 / 0.025 = −56.0 kJ mol⁻¹ (negative because exothermic)
Sources of Error in Calorimetry
Error sourceEffectImprovement
Heat loss to surroundingsΔT smaller than expected → ΔH underestimatedUse insulated cup; plot temp-time graph and extrapolate
Heat capacity of cup ignoredΔH slightly underestimatedUse calibrated calorimeter; account for Ccal
Incomplete combustionLess heat releasedEnsure good oxygen supply
Density of solution assumed = waterSmall error in massWeigh the solution directly
18.7

Born-Haber Cycles

A Born-Haber cycle is a specific application of Hess's Law used to calculate lattice enthalpy — a quantity that cannot be measured directly. It applies Hess's Law to ionic compound formation, breaking the process into measurable steps.

StepNameEnergy termTypical sign
1Atomisation of metalΔH°at(metal)+ (endothermic)
2Atomisation of non-metal (e.g., ½Cl₂ → Cl)ΔH°at(non-metal)+ (endothermic)
31st ionisation energy of metalIE₁+ (endothermic)
42nd ionisation energy (if M²⁺)IE₂+ (endothermic)
5Electron affinity of non-metalΔH°ea− (usually exothermic)
6Lattice enthalpy (formation)ΔH°latt− (exothermic)
ResultOverall formation enthalpyΔH°fUsually −
Hess's Law applied to Born-Haber
For NaClΔH°f[NaCl] = ΔH°at[Na] + ΔH°at[½Cl₂] + IE₁[Na] + ΔH°ea[Cl] + ΔH°latt[NaCl]

To find the unknown (usually ΔH°latt), rearrange the equation:

ΔH°latt = ΔH°f − ΔH°at[Na] − ΔH°at[½Cl₂] − IE₁ − ΔH°ea
Worked Example — Born-Haber Cycle for NaCl

Data: ΔH°f[NaCl] = −411, ΔH°at[Na] = +107, ΔH°at[½Cl₂] = +121, IE₁[Na] = +496, ΔH°ea[Cl] = −349 (all kJ mol⁻¹)

1
Apply Hess's Law: ΔH°latt = ΔH°f − (all other steps)
2
Sum of known steps = +107 + 121 + 496 + (−349) = +375 kJ mol⁻¹
3
ΔH°latt = −411 − 375 = −786 kJ mol⁻¹

The large negative value confirms NaCl is a stable ionic compound — the lattice is held together by strong electrostatic forces.

Factors affecting lattice enthalpy magnitude

Lattice enthalpy becomes more negative (stronger lattice) when:

  • Ionic charge increases: MgO (−3791) >> NaCl (−787) because Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ carry more charge
  • Ionic radius decreases: ions closer together → stronger electrostatic attraction
18.8

Entropy and Gibbs Free Energy

Enthalpy alone cannot predict whether a reaction is spontaneous (will proceed without external energy input). A reaction is favoured by two factors: releasing energy (exothermic) and increasing disorder. The second factor is described by entropy (S).

Entropy (S)

Entropy is a measure of the disorder or randomness of a system. Units: J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹.

ChangeSign of ΔSExample
Solid → liquid → gas+ (disorder increases)H₂O(l) → H₂O(g)
Gas dissolved → ions in solution+ (more particles)HCl(g) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)
Gas + gas → solid− (disorder decreases)N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃
More moles of gas formed+CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂↑
Gibbs Free Energy (ΔG)

Gibbs free energy (ΔG) combines enthalpy and entropy into a single criterion for spontaneity.

Gibbs EquationΔG = ΔH − TΔS
ΔGMeaning
ΔG < 0 (negative)Reaction is spontaneous (feasible) under these conditions
ΔG = 0Reaction is at equilibrium
ΔG > 0 (positive)Reaction is non-spontaneous (reverse reaction is favoured)

T is temperature in Kelvin. Note: ΔH is in kJ mol⁻¹ but ΔS is in J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ — convert to kJ before substituting!

ΔHΔSΔG = ΔH − TΔSSpontaneity
− (exo)+Always −Always spontaneous at all T
+ (endo)−Always +Never spontaneous
−−Depends on TSpontaneous at low T
++Depends on TSpontaneous at high T
Worked Example — Gibbs Free Energy

Is the decomposition of CaCO₃ spontaneous at 800 °C?

CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g)   ΔH° = +178 kJ mol⁻¹, ΔS° = +165 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹

1
T = 800 + 273 = 1073 K
2
Convert ΔS to kJ: +165 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ = +0.165 kJ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹
3
TΔS = 1073 × 0.165 = +177.0 kJ mol⁻¹
4
ΔG = +178 − 177 = +1 kJ mol⁻¹ ≈ 0 → barely non-spontaneous at 800 °C
5
At 900 °C (1173 K): ΔG = 178 − (1173 × 0.165) = 178 − 193.5 = −15.5 kJ mol⁻¹ → spontaneous ✓

This explains why limestone kilns operate above ~840 °C — only above this temperature does decomposition become spontaneous.

Q1

Define enthalpy change (ΔH) and state its units.

ΔH is the heat energy transferred between system and surroundings at constant pressure. Units: kJ mol⁻¹.
Q2

Is the sign of ΔH positive or negative for an exothermic reaction? Explain.

Negative (−). The products have less enthalpy than the reactants, so Hproducts − Hreactants < 0. Heat flows out to the surroundings.
Q3

What are the standard conditions for ΔH° measurements?

298 K (25 °C), 100 kPa pressure, all substances in their standard states.
Q4

State Hess's Law.

The total enthalpy change for a reaction is the same regardless of the route taken, provided the initial and final conditions are the same.
Q5

Write the formula for ΔH°rxn using standard enthalpies of formation.

ΔH°rxn = Σ ΔH°f(products) − Σ ΔH°f(reactants)
Q6

What is the standard enthalpy of formation of O₂(g)? Explain why.

Zero. By definition, ΔH°f of any element in its standard state is 0 — no reaction is needed to form it from itself.
Q7

Define standard enthalpy of combustion.

The enthalpy change when 1 mol of a substance is completely burned in excess oxygen under standard conditions.
Q8

What is activation energy? Mark it on a sketch of an energy profile diagram.

Activation energy (Eₐ) is the minimum energy that colliding particles must possess to react. On the diagram it is the energy difference from the reactant energy level up to the peak (transition state).
Q9

How does a catalyst affect the energy profile diagram? Does it change ΔH?

A catalyst lowers the activation energy (lowers the peak), providing an alternative reaction pathway. It does NOT change ΔH — the reactant and product energy levels remain unchanged.
Q10

When using bond enthalpies to calculate ΔH, which gives a positive contribution and which gives a negative contribution?

Breaking bonds (reactants) gives a positive contribution (endothermic). Forming bonds (products) gives a negative contribution (exothermic). ΔH = Σ(bonds broken) − Σ(bonds formed).
Q11

Why do bond enthalpy calculations give only approximate values of ΔH?

Bond enthalpy values are average values across many different compounds. The actual bond energy in a specific molecule may differ from the average.
Q12

Write the equation for calorimetry and define each term.

q = mcΔT. q = heat energy (J); m = mass of solution (g); c = specific heat capacity of water = 4.18 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹; ΔT = temperature change in °C.
Q13

50 cm³ of HCl is mixed with 50 cm³ of NaOH and the temperature rises by 5.0 °C. Calculate the heat released.

m = 100 g, c = 4.18 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹, ΔT = 5.0 °C → q = 100 × 4.18 × 5.0 = 2090 J = 2.09 kJ
Q14

Give three sources of error in a calorimetry experiment and how each can be reduced.

(1) Heat loss to surroundings → use insulated cup, extrapolate temp-time graph. (2) Heat capacity of cup ignored → use calibrated calorimeter. (3) Density assumed = 1 g cm⁻³ → weigh the solution directly.
Q15

What is lattice enthalpy? Why is it always negative when defined as ionic solid formation?

Lattice enthalpy is the enthalpy change when 1 mol of ionic compound is formed from its gaseous ions. It is exothermic (negative) because strong electrostatic attractions are formed between oppositely charged ions.
Q16

Name all the steps in a Born-Haber cycle for NaCl, starting from the elements.

(1) Atomisation of Na(s) → Na(g). (2) Atomisation of ½Cl₂(g) → Cl(g). (3) 1st ionisation energy of Na(g) → Na⁺(g) + e⁻. (4) Electron affinity: Cl(g) + e⁻ → Cl⁻(g). (5) Lattice enthalpy: Na⁺(g) + Cl⁻(g) → NaCl(s).
Q17

MgO has a much more negative lattice enthalpy than NaCl. Explain why in terms of ionic charges and radii.

Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ carry double the charge of Na⁺ and Cl⁻, so electrostatic attractions are much stronger. Also, Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ have smaller ionic radii, bringing ions closer together and further strengthening the lattice.
Q18

Define entropy. Give two examples of changes that increase entropy.

Entropy is a measure of the disorder or randomness of a system. Examples: (1) liquid water vaporising to steam; (2) a solid dissolving into ions in solution.
Q19

Write the Gibbs free energy equation and state when a reaction is spontaneous.

ΔG = ΔH − TΔS. A reaction is spontaneous when ΔG < 0 (negative).
Q20

A reaction has ΔH = +200 kJ mol⁻¹ and ΔS = +500 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. At what temperature does it become spontaneous?

ΔG = 0 at the crossover point. 0 = ΔH − TΔS → T = ΔH/ΔS = 200 000 J / 500 J K⁻¹ = 400 K (127 °C). Above 400 K, ΔG becomes negative and the reaction is spontaneous.
Q21

ΔH = −100 kJ mol⁻¹, ΔS = −200 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. Is this reaction always, never, or sometimes spontaneous?

Sometimes spontaneous — at LOW temperatures. Since ΔH < 0 and ΔS < 0, ΔG = ΔH − TΔS becomes less negative (less favourable) as T rises. It is spontaneous when T < ΔH/ΔS = 100 000/200 = 500 K.
Q22

Using ΔH°f data, calculate ΔH° for: 2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2SO₃(g). Given: ΔH°f[SO₂] = −297, ΔH°f[SO₃] = −396 kJ mol⁻¹.

ΔH° = Σproducts − Σreactants = [2×(−396)] − [2×(−297) + 0] = −792 − (−594) = −198 kJ mol⁻¹
Q23

Using bond enthalpies, calculate ΔH for H₂(g) + Cl₂(g) → 2HCl(g). [H–H: 436, Cl–Cl: 243, H–Cl: 432 kJ mol⁻¹]

Bonds broken: 1×H–H + 1×Cl–Cl = 436 + 243 = 679 kJ. Bonds formed: 2×H–Cl = 2×432 = 864 kJ. ΔH = 679 − 864 = −185 kJ mol⁻¹
Q24

Distinguish between the transition state and an intermediate in a reaction mechanism.

The transition state is the highest-energy point in a single step — it is unstable, exists momentarily, and cannot be isolated. An intermediate exists in a trough between two peaks on a multi-step profile; it has finite lifetime and can sometimes be detected.
Q25

A weak acid neutralisation produces ΔHneut = −52 kJ mol⁻¹, compared to −57.3 kJ mol⁻¹ for HCl/NaOH. Explain the difference.

Weak acids are partially dissociated. Energy must be supplied to fully ionise the weak acid before neutralisation can occur. This ionisation is endothermic, so the net enthalpy released is less than for a strong acid where full ionisation has already occurred.

C1 [5]

A student claims that all exothermic reactions are spontaneous and all endothermic reactions are non-spontaneous. Evaluate this claim using the Gibbs free energy equation, with one worked numerical example to illustrate your argument.

[1] The claim is incorrect — spontaneity depends on ΔG = ΔH − TΔS, not ΔH alone. [1] An endothermic reaction CAN be spontaneous if ΔS is sufficiently positive and T is high enough (e.g., CaCO₃ decomposition above ~840 °C). [1] An exothermic reaction can be non-spontaneous if ΔS is very negative and T is high (e.g., N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ at very high T). [1] Numerical example: CaCO₃ decomposition — ΔH = +178, ΔS = +165 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹; at 1200 K, ΔG = 178 − 1200×0.165 = 178 − 198 = −20 kJ mol⁻¹ → spontaneous despite being endothermic [1].
C2 [5]

Describe the experimental procedure for measuring the enthalpy change of the reaction between zinc powder and copper sulfate solution using calorimetry. Include all steps, sources of error, and how you would calculate ΔH per mole of zinc.

[1] Measure 25.0 cm³ of known concentration CuSO₄ into an insulated polystyrene cup; record initial temperature. [1] Add excess zinc powder (weighed), stir, and record maximum temperature reached. [1] Calculate q = mcΔT (m = mass of solution, c = 4.18 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹, ΔT = Tmax − Tinitial). [1] Calculate moles of CuSO₄ (the limiting reagent) = c × V; ΔH = −q / n(CuSO₄) in kJ mol⁻¹ (negative because exothermic). [1] Sources of error: heat loss to surroundings (reduce: insulate cup, extrapolate temp-time graph); heat capacity of cup ignored (reduce: use calibrated calorimeter); zinc may not react completely (reduce: use excess zinc).
