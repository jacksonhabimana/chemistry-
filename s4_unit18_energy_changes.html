18.1

Internal Energy and the First Law of Thermodynamics

Internal Energy (U)

The internal energy (U) of a system is the total energy stored within it ‚ÄĒ the sum of all kinetic energies (translational, rotational, vibrational) and potential energies (intermolecular, chemical bonds) of all particles in the system.

First Law of Thermodynamics Energy cannot be created or destroyed ‚ÄĒ it can only be transferred or converted from one form to another. The total energy of the universe is constant.

őĒU = q + w
where őĒU = change in internal energy, q = heat transferred to system, w = work done on system

In chemistry, most reactions occur at constant pressure (open to the atmosphere), so we use enthalpy (H) rather than internal energy.

18.2

Enthalpy and Enthalpy Change (őĒH)

Enthalpy (H)

Enthalpy is defined as: H = U + pV (internal energy + pressure √ó volume). For reactions at constant pressure, the heat exchanged equals the enthalpy change:

őĒH = H(products) ‚ąí H(reactants) őĒH < 0 ‚Üí exothermic (heat released to surroundings) őĒH > 0 ‚Üí endothermic (heat absorbed from surroundings) Units: kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ (per mole of reaction as written)
ūüß†
Sign convention ‚ÄĒ always from the system's perspective Exothermic: system loses energy ‚Üí őĒH is NEGATIVE (‚ąí). The surroundings get warmer.
Endothermic: system gains energy ‚Üí őĒH is POSITIVE (+). The surroundings get cooler.
18.3

Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

Exothermic Reactions

Reactions that release heat to the surroundings. The temperature of the surroundings increases. Products have lower enthalpy than reactants.

Examples of exothermic reactions: ‚ÄĘ Combustion: CH‚āĄ + 2O‚āā ‚Üí CO‚āā + 2H‚āāO őĒH = ‚ąí890 kJ/mol ‚ÄĘ Neutralisation: HCl + NaOH ‚Üí NaCl + H‚āāO őĒH = ‚ąí57.1 kJ/mol ‚ÄĘ Respiration: C‚āÜH‚āĀ‚āāO‚āÜ + 6O‚āā ‚Üí 6CO‚āā + 6H‚āāO őĒH = ‚ąí2803 kJ/mol ‚ÄĘ Formation MgO: 2Mg + O‚āā ‚Üí 2MgO őĒH = ‚ąí1204 kJ/mol ‚ÄĘ Oxidation Fe: 4Fe + 3O‚āā ‚Üí 2Fe‚āāO‚āÉ őĒH = ‚ąí1648 kJ/mol

Endothermic Reactions

Reactions that absorb heat from the surroundings. The temperature of the surroundings decreases. Products have higher enthalpy than reactants.

Examples of endothermic reactions: ‚ÄĘ Thermal decomp: CaCO‚āÉ ‚Üí CaO + CO‚āā őĒH = +178 kJ/mol ‚ÄĘ Photosynthesis: 6CO‚āā + 6H‚āāO ‚Üí C‚āÜH‚āĀ‚āāO‚āÜ + 6O‚āā őĒH = +2803 kJ/mol ‚ÄĘ Dissolving NH‚āĄNO‚āÉ: NH‚āĄNO‚āÉ(s) ‚Üí NH‚āĄ‚Āļ(aq)+NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ(aq) őĒH = +25.7 kJ/mol ‚ÄĘ Evaporation: H‚āāO(l) ‚Üí H‚āāO(g) őĒH = +44 kJ/mol
ūüí°
Practical tests for exo/endothermic Exothermic: test tube feels HOT; thermometer rises. Endothermic: test tube feels COLD; thermometer drops. Dissolving ammonium nitrate in water is a classic endothermic demonstration ‚ÄĒ the beaker becomes ice-cold.

18.5

Hess's Law

Hess's Law (1840) The total enthalpy change for a chemical reaction is independent of the route taken, provided the initial and final conditions are the same. őĒH is a state function ‚ÄĒ it depends only on start and end states, not on the pathway.

Using Hess's Law ‚ÄĒ Two Methods

Method 1: Enthalpy Cycle (Route A = Route B)

őĒH¬įreaction = ő£őĒH¬įf(products) ‚ąí ő£őĒH¬įf(reactants) Example: CH‚āĄ + 2O‚āā ‚Üí CO‚āā + 2H‚āāO őĒH¬į = [őĒH¬įf(CO‚āā) + 2√óőĒH¬įf(H‚āāO)] ‚ąí [őĒH¬įf(CH‚āĄ) + 2√óőĒH¬įf(O‚āā)] = [‚ąí394 + 2√ó(‚ąí286)] ‚ąí [‚ąí74.8 + 2√ó(0)] = ‚ąí966 ‚ąí (‚ąí74.8) = ‚ąí891.2 kJ/mol ‚úď (matches direct measurement)

Method 2: Algebraic (adding equations)

Reactions can be added, reversed (flip sign of őĒH), or multiplied (scale őĒH) to give the target equation.

18.7

Energy Profile Diagrams

Energy Profile for Exothermic Reactions

In an exothermic reaction, the products have lower enthalpy than the reactants. The curve rises to a peak (the transition state / activated complex) then falls to a lower level.

Energy Profile for Endothermic Reactions

In an endothermic reaction, products have higher enthalpy than reactants. The curve rises to a peak then falls to a level still above the starting point.

Effect of a Catalyst on the Energy Profile

A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. It does NOT change őĒH (same reactants and products ‚Üí same enthalpy difference). On the diagram, the peak is lower but the start and end levels stay the same.

Without catalyst: Ea = large ‚Üí slow reaction With catalyst: Ea = smaller ‚Üí faster reaction őĒH: UNCHANGED by catalyst Kc: UNCHANGED by catalyst (only affects rate, not equilibrium)

őĒH < 0 ‚Üí exothermic. Products are at lower enthalpy than reactants. Energy is released to the surroundings ‚Üí surroundings get warmer. Examples: combustion, neutralisation, respiration. Endothermic reactions have őĒH > 0.

When a catalyst is added to a reaction at equilibrium, which quantity changes?

A catalyst lowers the ACTIVATION ENERGY (Ea) by providing an alternative pathway. It does NOT change őĒH, Kc, the enthalpy of reactants or products, or the equilibrium position. Only temperature changes Kc. A catalyst makes the reaction reach equilibrium faster, but at the same position.

A catalyst lowers Ea ‚Üí more molecules exceed Ea ‚Üí faster reaction. Ea is always positive (energy input needed to reach transition state).

Q15

The sign convention for őĒH is based on:

őĒH sign is from the SYSTEM's perspective. Exothermic: system loses heat ‚Üí őĒH negative. Endothermic: system gains heat ‚Üí őĒH positive. The surroundings do the opposite: if őĒH is negative, the surroundings gain heat (temperature rises). Note: őĒT positive ‚ÜĎ means exothermic (surroundings gain heat), but őĒH is negative (system loses heat).
Q16

Photosynthesis is an example of which type of reaction?

Photosynthesis: 6CO‚āā + 6H‚āāO ‚Üí C‚āÜH‚āĀ‚āāO‚āÜ + 6O‚āā, őĒH = +2803 kJ/mol. ENDOTHERMIC ‚ÄĒ absorbs light energy (from the sun) to convert low-energy CO‚āā and H‚āāO into high-energy glucose. This is why plants need sunlight. Respiration (the reverse) is exothermic and releases the same energy.
Q17

In Hess's Law, if you reverse a reaction equation, you must:

Reversing a reaction equation changes the sign of őĒH (multiply by ‚ąí1). If A ‚Üí B has őĒH = ‚ąí100 kJ/mol, then B ‚Üí A has őĒH = +100 kJ/mol. Scaling: if you multiply the equation by n, multiply őĒH by n too. This is the foundation of Hess's law algebra.
Q18

Standard conditions for thermochemistry are:

Standard conditions (IUPAC): 298 K (25¬įC), 100 kPa pressure (1 bar), all substances in standard states (most stable physical form at 298 K and 100 kPa), solutions at 1 mol/dm¬≥. Note: 100 kPa ‚Čą 1 bar (not 1 atm = 101.325 kPa). Standard conditions are marked with ¬į symbol: őĒH¬į.
Q19

Which of the following has the largest activation energy?

High Ea ‚Üí slow reaction requiring much energy to get started. A reaction needing 800¬įC has a very high Ea ‚ÄĒ most molecules don't have enough energy at room temperature. Explosive reactions have low Ea (once started, chain reaction proceeds rapidly). Enzyme-catalysed reactions have low Ea (enzyme lowers it). Ionic reactions in solution (e.g. precipitation) have virtually zero Ea.
Q20

The enthalpy change calculated using bond enthalpies differs from Hess's law result because:

Two reasons: (1) Bond enthalpies are AVERAGE values from many different molecules ‚ÄĒ actual bond strength varies with molecular environment. (2) Bond enthalpy method assumes all species are in the GAS PHASE. If any reactants/products are liquid or solid, intermolecular forces must also be overcome/formed ‚ÄĒ but bond enthalpies ignore this. Hess's law uses precise experimental őĒHf¬į values.
Q21

Which statement about the enthalpy of atomisation is correct?

Enthalpy of atomisation is ALWAYS positive (endothermic). Energy must be supplied to convert a substance in its standard state to isolated gaseous atoms. For metals (Na, Fe, etc.): atomisation = sublimation ‚Üí endothermic. For Cl‚āā: ¬ĹCl‚āā(g) ‚Üí Cl(g), őĒH¬įat = +121 kJ/mol (half the Cl‚ÄďCl bond enthalpy). Used in Born-Haber cycles.
Q22

A student measures őĒH for a reaction using a polystyrene cup. The value obtained is always less exothermic than the true value because:

Heat always leaks to the surroundings during a calorimetry experiment, even with a polystyrene cup (good insulator but not perfect). The measured őĒT is lower than the true őĒT ‚Üí q calculated is smaller ‚Üí őĒH is less negative than it should be. Ways to reduce this: use a lid, measure quickly, extrapolate the temperature-time graph to find the true maximum T.
Q23

The standard enthalpy of combustion is always:

Combustion is ALWAYS exothermic ‚ÄĒ burning releases energy. Therefore őĒH¬įc is always NEGATIVE. The products (CO‚āā, H‚āāO) are at lower enthalpy than the reactants (fuel + O‚āā). This is why fuels are useful ‚ÄĒ they release energy on burning. No fuel has a positive enthalpy of combustion.
Q24

What does the area under the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution curve to the right of Ea represent?

The shaded area to the RIGHT of Ea on the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution represents the fraction of molecules with energy ‚Č• Ea ‚ÄĒ these are the molecules that can successfully react when they collide. Higher temperature ‚Üí the curve shifts right ‚Üí larger area to the right of Ea ‚Üí more successful collisions ‚Üí faster rate. Catalyst lowers Ea ‚Üí more of the curve is to the right of Ea ‚Üí faster reaction at same temperature.
Q25

őĒH¬įf for water is ‚ąí286 kJ/mol. This means:

őĒH¬įf(H‚āāO,l) = ‚ąí286 kJ/mol means: H‚āā(g) + ¬ĹO‚āā(g) ‚Üí H‚āāO(l), őĒH¬į = ‚ąí286 kJ/mol. This refers to LIQUID water at standard conditions. 286 kJ of energy are RELEASED (exothermic, negative sign) per mole of liquid water formed. Note: őĒH¬įf(H‚āāO,g) = ‚ąí242 kJ/mol (less negative because energy needed for vaporisation not released).

Q1 [5 marks]

State and explain the First Law of Thermodynamics. Define internal energy and enthalpy. Explain why chemists use enthalpy rather than internal energy to describe most reactions. [5]

First Law: Energy cannot be created or destroyed ‚ÄĒ only transferred or converted. The total energy of the universe is constant. őĒU = q + w. [1] Internal energy (U): total of all kinetic and potential energies of all particles in a system (translational, rotational, vibrational KE + intermolecular PE + chemical bond PE). [1] Enthalpy (H = U + pV): at constant pressure, őĒH = heat exchanged. Most reactions occur at constant pressure (atmosphere), so heat measured = őĒH. [1] Why H not U: if a reaction is carried out in a sealed container (constant volume), őĒU = q. But most lab reactions are open to the atmosphere at constant pressure. Work done by gas expansion (pőĒV) is automatically accounted for in őĒH, making it more practical. őĒH = őĒU + pőĒV. [2]
