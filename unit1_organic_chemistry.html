1.1

Classification of Organic Compounds

What is Organic Chemistry?

Organic chemistry is the branch of chemistry that deals with the study of carbon-containing compounds. The word "organic" originally referred to substances obtained from living organisms, but today millions of synthetic organic compounds are known.

Carbon is unique because it can form 4 covalent bonds and can bond to itself to form long chains, rings, and branched structures — a property called catenation.

Definition An organic compound is any compound that contains carbon atoms covalently bonded to hydrogen, and often to oxygen, nitrogen, sulfur, halogens, or other elements.
ℹ️
Exceptions to the carbon rule Some carbon-containing compounds are NOT classified as organic: CO₂ (carbon dioxide), CO (carbon monoxide), carbonates (e.g. CaCO₃), bicarbonates, and cyanides (CN⁻) are considered inorganic.

A: Classification by Carbon Skeleton (Chain Structure)

Organic compounds are first grouped by the shape of their carbon backbone:

CyclicContains at least one ring of carbon atomsCyclohexane, benzeneIncludes aromatic and non-aromatic rings
AlicyclicCyclic but NOT aromaticCyclohexane C₆H₁₂Behave like aliphatic compounds
AromaticContains a benzene ring (C₆H₆)Benzene, toluene, phenolHighly stable due to delocalized π electrons
HeterocyclicRing contains atoms other than C (e.g. N, O, S)Pyridine (N in ring), furan (O in ring)Very common in medicines and DNA bases

B: Classification by Degree of Unsaturation

Organic compounds are also classified by the type of bonds between carbon atoms:

ClassBondsGeneral FormulaExamples
SaturatedC–C single bonds onlyCₙH₂ₙ₊₂ (alkanes)Methane CH₄, ethane C₂H₆
UnsaturatedC=C double or C≡C triple bonds presentCₙH₂ₙ (alkenes), CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ (alkynes)Ethene C₂H₄, ethyne C₂H₂
💡
Quick Memory Tip Saturated = no more hydrogen can be added (like saturated fat — full!). Unsaturated = still has room for more hydrogen (can react with H₂ in addition reactions).

1.1 Classification of Organic Compounds
10 Questions
Q1
Organic chemistry is the study of:
Organic chemistry deals with carbon-containing compounds. Carbon must be covalently bonded to hydrogen. Exceptions include CO₂, CO, carbonates, and cyanides which are classified as inorganic.
Q2
The unique ability of carbon to bond to itself to form long chains and rings is called:
Catenation is the property of carbon to form covalent bonds with other carbon atoms, producing long chains, branched structures, and rings. This is why millions of organic compounds exist.
Q3
Which of the following is NOT classified as an organic compound?
CaCO₃ is an inorganic carbonate. Exceptions to the organic classification include CO₂, CO, carbonates, bicarbonates, and cyanides — despite containing carbon.
Q4
An acyclic organic compound is one that:
Acyclic (also called aliphatic) compounds have carbon atoms arranged in open chains with no ring. Examples: butane, pentane, ethanol.
Q5
Aromatic organic compounds are defined by containing:
Aromatic compounds contain a benzene ring. They are highly stable due to the 6 delocalised π electrons spread over all 6 carbons. Examples: benzene, toluene, phenol.
Q6
A heterocyclic compound contains:
Heterocyclic compounds have rings containing atoms other than carbon, such as nitrogen (pyridine), oxygen (furan), or sulfur (thiophene). They are very common in medicines and DNA bases.
Q7
Saturated organic compounds contain:
Saturated compounds have only single C–C bonds (e.g. alkanes, CₙH₂ₙ₊₂). No more hydrogen can be added. Unsaturated compounds have C=C or C≡C bonds.
Q8
Which general formula represents an unsaturated compound with one C=C double bond?
CₙH₂ₙ is the general formula for alkenes — one C=C double bond. CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ = alkanes (saturated), CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ = alkynes (one C≡C).
Q9
Alicyclic compounds are:
Alicyclic compounds (e.g. cyclohexane C₆H₁₂) contain rings but not benzene rings. They behave chemically like aliphatic (open-chain) compounds.
Q10
Carbon can form 4 covalent bonds. This means carbon is:
Carbon has 4 valence electrons and forms exactly 4 covalent bonds (tetravalent). This allows it to bond to H, O, N, halogens, and other C atoms in many combinations.

Types of Formulas for Organic Compounds

There are four main ways to represent an organic molecule. Each gives different levels of information.

Type of FormulaWhat it showsExample: Butane
Molecular (Molecular Formula) The total number of each type of atom in one molecule C₄H₁₀
Empirical Formula The simplest whole-number ratio of atoms C₂H₅
Structural Formula Shows how atoms are connected (all bonds shown) CH₃–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃
Displayed (Full Structural) Formula Shows every atom and every bond explicitly All H atoms drawn out around each C
Skeletal Formula Zigzag lines; C atoms at each corner/end; H atoms implied A zigzag with 3 line segments
Condensed Structural Formula Groups H atoms with each carbon, reading left to right CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃
// Butane: Different representations compared

Molecular: C₄H₁₀
Empirical: C₂H₅
Condensed: CH₃–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃
IUPAC name: butane

Relationship between Molecular and Empirical Formulas

The molecular formula is always a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula.

If empirical formula is CH₂ and the molar mass is 56 g/mol:

Mass of CH₂ unit = 12 + 2 = 14 g/mol  →   n = 56 ÷ 14 = 4

So molecular formula = (CH₂)₄ = C₄H₈ (this is butene or cyclobutane)

1.2 Types of Formulas
10 Questions
Q1
The molecular formula of a compound shows:
The molecular formula (e.g. C₄H₁₀ for butane) gives the actual number of each element in one molecule. It does not show bonding or structure.
Q2
The empirical formula of butane (C₄H₁₀) is:
The empirical formula is the simplest whole-number ratio. C₄H₁₀: divide by 2 → C₂H₅. It cannot be simplified further (2 and 5 share no common factor).
Q3
A structural formula shows:
The structural formula (also called displayed or full structural formula) shows every bond (C–H, C–C, etc.) between all atoms, making the connectivity clear.
Q4
A skeletal (line-angle) formula represents:
In skeletal formulas, each bend/end of a line = a carbon. Hydrogens on carbons are not shown — assumed to complete carbon's 4 bonds. Functional groups and heteroatoms ARE shown.
Q5
The condensed structural formula CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃ represents:
CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃ = 4 carbons in a row = butane (C₄H₁₀). Condensed formulas show the carbon chain with H atoms grouped, without drawing each bond.
Q6
Which formula type gives the MOST information about a molecule?
The structural (displayed) formula shows all atoms AND all bonds, revealing both the composition and connectivity. Molecular formula shows only composition.
Q7
Glucose has molecular formula C₆H₁₂O₆. Its empirical formula is:
Simplify C₆H₁₂O₆ by dividing all by 6: CH₂O. This is the empirical formula — the simplest whole-number ratio (1:2:1 for C:H:O).
Q8
Two compounds can have the same molecular formula but different structural formulas. These are called:
Isomers have the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements. For example, butane and methylpropane both have C₄H₁₀ but different carbon skeletons.
Q9
In a skeletal formula, a straight-line segment represents:
Each line segment in a skeletal formula represents a C–C bond. The carbon at each end is understood; its remaining bonds are to (implied) hydrogen atoms.
Q10
The general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ represents which homologous series?
CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ is the general formula for alkanes — saturated hydrocarbons. For n=1: CH₄ (methane); n=2: C₂H₆ (ethane); n=3: C₃H₈ (propane).
1.3

Functional Groups and Homologous Series

Functional Group A functional group is an atom or group of atoms within a molecule that is responsible for its characteristic chemical reactions. The rest of the molecule (usually a carbon chain) is called the alkyl group (R–).
Homologous Series A homologous series is a family of organic compounds that (i) have the same functional group, (ii) differ by a –CH₂– unit from one member to the next, (iii) have the same general formula, (iv) show a gradual change in physical properties, and (v) have similar chemical properties.

Esters–COO–CₙH₂ₙO₂Methyl ethanoate CH₃COOCH₃

1.3 Functional Groups & Homologous Series
10 Questions
Q1
A functional group is:
The functional group determines the chemical behaviour of the molecule. The rest of the molecule (the alkyl group, R–) does not change the characteristic reactions.
Q2
Members of a homologous series differ from each other by:
Each consecutive member of a homologous series has one more –CH₂– unit (14 mass units) than the previous. They share the same functional group and general formula.
Q3
The functional group of alcohols is:
Alcohols contain the –OH (hydroxyl) functional group. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH. Example: ethanol C₂H₅OH.
Q4
Which functional group identifies a carboxylic acid?
Carboxylic acids contain –COOH. General formula: CₙH₂ₙO₂. Example: ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH). The –COOH group acts as both a C=O and an –OH.
Q5
Alkenes belong to a homologous series with which functional group?
Alkenes contain the C=C functional group (double bond). General formula: CₙH₂ₙ. Example for n=2: ethene C₂H₄.
Q6
Why do boiling points increase as chain length increases within a homologous series?
As chain length increases, the number of electrons increases → stronger London dispersion (van der Waals) forces between molecules → higher boiling point. All members share the same functional group.
Q7
Aldehydes and ketones both have the general formula CₙH₂ₙO and contain:
Both aldehydes (–CHO, where C=O is at the end of the chain) and ketones (–CO–, where C=O is in the middle) contain the carbonyl group C=O.
Q8
The functional group –NH₂ identifies:
Amines contain the –NH₂ (amino) group. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₁NH₂. Example: ethylamine C₂H₅NH₂. They are organic derivatives of ammonia.
Q9
Members of the same homologous series have:
A homologous series has: (i) same functional group → similar chemistry; (ii) differ by –CH₂– → gradual change in physical properties (bp, mp, density, solubility).
Q10
The ester functional group is:
Esters contain the –COO– linkage (–CO–O–). General formula: CₙH₂ₙO₂. Example: methyl ethanoate CH₃COOCH₃. Formed from carboxylic acid + alcohol.
1.4

General Rules of IUPAC Nomenclature

What is IUPAC?

IUPAC stands for the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry. Their naming system provides a unique, systematic name for every organic compound that conveys its structure.

The Three Building Blocks of an IUPAC Name

Every IUPAC name has up to three parts: PREFIX + ROOT/STEM + SUFFIX

PartTells you…Example
PrefixNames and positions of substituents (branches, halogens)2-methyl, 3-chloro
Root/StemThe length of the longest carbon chainmeth(1), eth(2), prop(3), but(4), pent(5), hex(6), hept(7), oct(8), non(9), dec(10)
SuffixThe main functional group (class of compound)-ane, -ene, -yne, -ol, -al, -one, -oic acid

Step-by-Step IUPAC Naming Rules

Rule 1 — Find the longest chain: Identify the longest continuous chain of carbon atoms that contains the principal functional group. This gives the root name (stem).

Rule 2 — Number the chain: Number the carbon atoms from the end closest to the principal functional group (or the branch, if no functional group). The functional group / substituents must get the lowest possible locants (numbers).

Rule 3 — Name substituents: Identify all substituents attached to the main chain (branches = alkyl groups; halogens, etc.). Use prefixes: methyl (–CH₃), ethyl (–C₂H₅), propyl (–C₃H₇), fluoro (–F), chloro (–Cl), bromo (–Br), iodo (–I).

Rule 4 — Multiple identical substituents: Use di-, tri-, tetra- when the same substituent appears 2, 3, 4 times. List their positions separately: 2,2-dimethyl (not 2-dimethyl).

Rule 5 — Alphabetical order: When there are two or more different substituents, list them alphabetically (ignore di-, tri-, etc. when alphabetising).

Rule 6 — Suffix for class: End the name with the correct suffix: –ane (alkane), –ene (alkene), –yne (alkyne), –ol (alcohol), –al (aldehyde), –one (ketone), –oic acid (carboxylic acid), –oate (ester), –amine (amine).

1.4 IUPAC Nomenclature
10 Questions
Q1
IUPAC stands for:
IUPAC = International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry. Their systematic naming rules give every organic compound a unique name that reveals its structure.
Q2
In an IUPAC name, the ROOT (stem) of the name tells you:
The root/stem is derived from the number of carbons in the longest chain: meth(1), eth(2), prop(3), but(4), pent(5), hex(6), hept(7), oct(8), non(9), dec(10).
Q3
The suffix –ol in an IUPAC name indicates:
The suffix shows the principal functional group. –ol = alcohol; –al = aldehyde; –one = ketone; –oic acid = carboxylic acid; –ane = alkane; –ene = alkene; –yne = alkyne.
Q4
When numbering the carbon chain for IUPAC naming, you start from the end that gives:
Rule 2: Number from the end closest to the principal functional group to give the lowest possible locants. If no functional group, number from the end closest to the first branch.
Q5
A compound has the name 2-methylpropane. How many carbons are in the longest chain?
'Prop' = 3 carbons in the longest chain. '2-methyl' = a methyl branch at carbon 2. Total carbons = 4 (3 in chain + 1 methyl), but the longest continuous chain = 3.
Q6
The prefix 'di-' in 2,3-dimethylbutane means:
Di- means two identical substituents. 2,3-dimethylbutane has two methyl groups: one at C2 and one at C3 of a butane (4-carbon) chain.
Q7
When two different substituents are present, they are listed in IUPAC names:
Rule 5: alphabetical order of substituent names, ignoring multiplying prefixes (di-, tri-). So 'bromo' comes before 'chloro'; 'ethyl' before 'methyl'.
Q8
The compound CH₃CH₂CH₂OH has the IUPAC name:
3-carbon chain (prop-), –OH at carbon 1 (–1-ol) → propan-1-ol. The hydroxyl group is at the end carbon (C1), giving the lowest locant.
Q9
The compound CH₃COCH₃ (propanone) has which functional group?
Propanone: 3-carbon chain, C=O at carbon 2 (middle) → ketone. The suffix is –one. IUPAC: propan-2-one (or propanone, since the 2 is the only possibility).
Q10
Which IUPAC suffix indicates a carboxylic acid?
The suffix –oic acid indicates a carboxylic acid (–COOH). Examples: methanoic acid (HCOOH), ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH), propanoic acid (CH₃CH₂COOH).

Worked Examples

Worked Example 1 — Naming a Branched Alkane

Question: Name the following compound: CH₃–CH(CH₃)–CH₂–CH₃

1
Find the longest chain. Count the main chain: C1–C2–C3–C4 = 4 carbons. Root = but-
2
Identify the substituent. Carbon 2 has an extra –CH₃ (methyl group) attached.
3
Number the chain. Number from the end closest to the branch: the branch is on C2 from the left OR C3 from the right. C2 is lower → number from left. Branch position = 2.
4
Write the name. Substituent (2-methyl) + root (but) + suffix (-ane).
Answer 2-methylbutane
Worked Example 2 — Drawing the Structure from a Name

Question: Draw the condensed structural formula of 3-ethyl-2-methylpentane.

1
Identify root: pent → 5 carbons in main chain.
C1–C2–C3–C4–C5
2
Identify substituents: 2-methyl (–CH₃ on C2), 3-ethyl (–C₂H₅ on C3).
3
Attach substituents to the main chain:
CH₃–CH(CH₃)–CH(C₂H₅)–CH₂–CH₃
4
Verify: count all carbons: 5 (chain) + 1 (methyl) + 2 (ethyl) = 8 carbons total → molecular formula C₈H₁₈.
Answer CH₃–CH(CH₃)–CH(C₂H₅)–CH₂–CH₃  |  C₈H₁₈
Worked Example 3 — Empirical vs Molecular Formula

Question: A hydrocarbon contains 85.7% carbon and 14.3% hydrogen by mass. Its molar mass is 56 g/mol. Determine the empirical and molecular formulas.

1
Find mole ratio. Assume 100 g sample:
Moles C = 85.7 ÷ 12 = 7.14 mol
Moles H = 14.3 ÷ 1 = 14.3 mol
2
Divide by smallest value (7.14):
C : H = 7.14/7.14 : 14.3/7.14 = 1 : 2
3
Empirical formula: CH₂
Empirical formula mass = 12 + 2 = 14 g/mol
4
Find n: n = 56 ÷ 14 = 4
Answer Empirical formula: CH₂  |  Molecular formula: C₄H₈
Worked Example 4 — Identifying the Functional Group & Homologous Series

Question: For each compound below, identify the functional group and state the homologous series it belongs to:
(a) CH₃CH₂OH    (b) CH₃COOH    (c) CH₃CHO    (d) CH₃CH₂NH₂

a
CH₃CH₂OH → functional group: –OH (hydroxyl) → Alcohol (ethanol)
b
CH₃COOH → functional group: –COOH (carboxyl) → Carboxylic acid (ethanoic acid)
c
CH₃CHO → functional group: –CHO (aldehyde) → Aldehyde (ethanal)
d
CH₃CH₂NH₂ → functional group: –NH₂ (amino) → Amine (ethylamine)
Summary (a) Alcohol  ·  (b) Carboxylic acid  ·  (c) Aldehyde  ·  (d) Amine
Worked Example 5 — Naming a Compound with Multiple Substituents

Question: Name: CH₃–C(CH₃)₂–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃

1
Longest chain: C1–C2–C3–C4–C5 = 5 carbons → pent-
2
C2 has two methyl groups attached → 2,2-dimethyl
3
Numbering: from left, branches at C2; from right, at C4. Left gives lower numbers → use left.
Answer 2,2-dimethylpentane

Exercise 1

Classify the following as saturated or unsaturated, and as aliphatic, aromatic, or alicyclic:
(a) C₆H₆    (b) C₄H₈    (c) C₃H₈    (d) Cyclohexane C₆H₁₂

(a) C₆H₆ → unsaturated, aromatic (benzene — contains a benzene ring with delocalized electrons)
(b) C₄H₈ → unsaturated, aliphatic (contains C=C double bond; open chain)
(c) C₃H₈ → saturated, aliphatic (propane — only C–C single bonds; open chain)
(d) C₆H₁₂ → saturated, alicyclic (cyclohexane — ring structure but no benzene ring)
Exercise 2

Give the IUPAC name for each compound:
(a) CH₃–CH₂–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃
(b) CH₃–CH(Cl)–CH₃
(c) CH₃–CH₂–OH
(d) CH₃–CH₂–COOH

(a) 5-carbon chain, no substituents, –ane suffix → pentane
(b) 3-carbon chain, Cl on C2, –ane suffix → 2-chloropropane
(c) 2-carbon chain, –OH group, –ol suffix → ethanol
(d) 3-carbon chain (including the carboxyl C), –oic acid suffix → propanoic acid
Exercise 3

A compound has the molecular formula C₄H₈O. It contains the functional group –CHO.
(a) What homologous series does it belong to?
(b) Write its IUPAC name.

(a) The –CHO functional group → Aldehydes
(b) 4-carbon chain + aldehyde suffix: butanal (CH₃CH₂CH₂CHO)
Exercise 4

A hydrocarbon has the empirical formula CH. Its molar mass is 78 g/mol. Find the molecular formula and identify the compound.

Empirical formula mass = 12 + 1 = 13 g/mol
n = 78 ÷ 13 = 6
Molecular formula = (CH)₆ = C₆H₆
This compound is benzene — an aromatic hydrocarbon with a ring structure and delocalized electrons.
Exercise 5

Write the condensed structural formula for:
(a) 2-methylpropane    (b) 3-methylhexane    (c) 2,3-dimethylbutane

(a) CH₃CH(CH₃)CH₃ — 4 carbons total, methyl branch on C2 of a 3-carbon chain (but IUPAC: rename — the longest chain is actually 4 → 2-methylpropane = isobutane)
(b) CH₃CH₂CH(CH₃)CH₂CH₂CH₃ — hexane chain (6C) + methyl on C3
(c) CH₃CH(CH₃)CH(CH₃)CH₃ — butane chain (4C) + methyl on C2 and C3
Exercise 6 — Extended

State four characteristics of a homologous series and illustrate each with an example from the alcohol series (CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH).

1. Same general formula: All alcohols fit CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH (e.g. methanol CH₃OH, ethanol C₂H₅OH, propanol C₃H₇OH)

2. Same functional group: All have the –OH (hydroxyl) group responsible for their chemical behaviour

3. Differ by –CH₂– unit: Methanol → ethanol → propanol each adds one –CH₂– (mass difference of 14 g/mol)

4. Gradual change in physical properties: Boiling points rise steadily: methanol 65°C, ethanol 78°C, propanol 97°C, butanol 118°C

5. Similar chemical properties: All alcohols undergo oxidation, esterification, and dehydration reactions

–COOH (carboxyl): a carbonyl (C=O) with an –OH on the same carbon. Gives acidic properties. e.g. ethanoic acid CH₃COOH.
Q19

As the number of carbon atoms in a homologous series increases, the boiling point:

More carbons → larger surface area → stronger London dispersion forces → higher boiling point. Smooth gradual trend in a homologous series.
Q20

The IUPAC name of CH₃–CH(OH)–CH₃ is:

3 carbons (prop-), –OH on carbon 2 (-2-ol) → propan-2-ol. Number from end that gives lowest locant to the –OH group.
Q21

A primary carbon atom is bonded to:

1°C: bonded to 1 C. 2°C: bonded to 2 C. 3°C: bonded to 3 C. 4°C (quaternary): bonded to 4 C. Affects reactivity.
Q22

Which pair are structural isomers?

Dimethyl ether and ethanol both have molecular formula C₂H₆O but different connectivity → structural isomers.
Q23

The prefix 'methyl-' in a branch name means:

Methyl (–CH₃) is a one-carbon substituent/branch. 2-methylpropane = propane main chain + CH₃ branch on C2.
Q24

Which statement correctly describes a condensed structural formula?

Condensed formula: atoms grouped by carbon but bonds not drawn (e.g. CH₃CH₂CH₃). Balance between detail and brevity.
Q25

The IUPAC name of CH₃CH₂COOH is:

3 carbons in the longest chain including the COOH carbon → prop-. Carboxylic acid suffix = -anoic acid → propanoic acid.
