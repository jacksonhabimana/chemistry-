3.1

Why Atoms Form Bonds

Atoms bond to achieve a more stable, lower-energy electron configuration — usually a full outer shell (octet, or duplet for H/He). Bonds form when the resulting arrangement has lower energy than the separate atoms.

Types of chemical bond
Bond typeFormed betweenTransfer or sharing
IonicMetal + non-metalTransfer of electrons
CovalentNon-metal + non-metalSharing of electron pairs
MetallicMetal + metalDelocalised electron sea
3.2

Ionic Bonding

Ionic bonds form when a metal transfers one or more electrons to a non-metal, forming oppositely charged ions that attract each other electrostatically.

Formation of NaCl

Na (2,8,1) → Na⁺ (2,8) + e⁻  |  Cl (2,8,7) + e⁻ → Cl⁻ (2,8,8). The ions arrange in a giant ionic lattice held by electrostatic forces in all directions.

PropertyExplanation
High melting/boiling pointStrong electrostatic forces between many ions require large energy to break
Conduct electricity when molten/dissolvedIons are free to move and carry charge
Do not conduct when solidIons are fixed in lattice positions
BrittleDisplacement of layers brings like charges together → repulsion → lattice shatters
Often soluble in waterPolar water molecules attract and surround ions (hydration)
3.3

Lattice Energy

Definition

Lattice energy (ΔH°latt) is the enthalpy change when 1 mol of an ionic compound is formed from its gaseous ions. Always exothermic (negative).

Na⁺(g) + Cl⁻(g) → NaCl(s)   ΔH°latt = −787 kJ mol⁻¹

Larger lattice energy (more negative) means a more stable ionic compound. Lattice energy is larger when: ions have higher charge (MgO >> NaCl) and when ions have smaller radius (closer together → stronger attraction).

3.4

Metallic Bonding

The Electron Sea Model

In metals, the outer electrons are delocalised — they leave their parent atoms and move freely through the structure. This leaves a lattice of positive metal ions surrounded by a "sea" of mobile electrons. The attraction between the positive ions and the delocalised electrons is the metallic bond.

PropertyExplanation
High melting/boiling pointStrong metallic bonds require large energy to break (especially transition metals)
Good electrical conductorsDelocalised electrons move freely and carry charge
Good thermal conductorsMobile electrons transfer kinetic energy rapidly
Malleable and ductileLayers of ions can slide over each other without breaking bonds; electron sea re-forms around new positions
Lustrous (shiny)Delocalised electrons absorb and re-emit light
3.5

Factors Affecting Metallic Bond Strength

FactorEffect on bond strengthExample
Number of delocalised electrons per atomMore electrons → stronger bondAl (3 e⁻) > Mg (2 e⁻) > Na (1 e⁻)
Ionic radiusSmaller ion → higher charge density → stronger bondMg²⁺ smaller than Na⁺
Charge on cationHigher charge → stronger attraction to electron seaAl³⁺ > Mg²⁺ > Na⁺

This is why aluminium has a higher melting point than magnesium, which has a higher melting point than sodium.

Conductivity (liquid)Yes — ions mobileYes — electrons still mobile
MalleabilityNo — brittleYes — malleable and ductile
