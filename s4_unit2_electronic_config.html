2.1

Bohr's Model and Emission Spectra

Niels Bohr (1913) proposed that electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed circular energy levels (shells). Electrons can only exist in these discrete energy levels, not between them.

How emission spectra are produced

When electrons absorb energy (heat or electricity), they jump to a higher energy level (excitation). When they fall back to a lower level, they release energy as a photon of light. The energy of the photon corresponds to the difference between the two levels: ΔE = hf.

SeriesElectrons fall to levelRegion of spectrum
Lymann=1 (ground state)Ultraviolet
Balmern=2Visible (hydrogen gives red, blue-green, blue, violet lines)
Paschenn=3Infrared

The visible emission spectrum of hydrogen shows four coloured lines — the Balmer series. Each line corresponds to electrons falling from a higher level to n=2.

2.3

Rules for Filling Orbitals

Three Key Rules

1. Aufbau principle — electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy: 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d …

2. Pauli exclusion principle — each orbital holds maximum 2 electrons with opposite spins (↑↓).

3. Hund's rule — when filling orbitals of equal energy (degenerate), electrons occupy separate orbitals with parallel spins before pairing up. This minimises electron-electron repulsion.

Energy order to remember: 1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d < 4p (note: 4s fills before 3d but 3d is emptied before 4s in ions)

For ions, remove electrons from the highest energy sub-shell first. For transition metals, remove 4s electrons before 3d electrons when forming cations.

Fe²⁺: [Ar] 3d⁶ (lost both 4s electrons)  |  Fe³⁺: [Ar] 3d⁵ (lost both 4s + one 3d)

Cl⁻: [Ne] 3s² 3p⁶ = [Ar] (gained one electron into 3p)

2.5

Ionisation Energies

Definition

First ionisation energy (IE₁) is the energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms to form one mole of gaseous 1+ ions.

X(g) → X⁺(g) + e⁻   ΔH = IE₁ (always positive)

Successive ionisation energies (IE₁, IE₂, IE₃ …) always increase because you are removing electrons from an increasingly positive ion.

Large jumps in successive IEs indicate a change of shell — this is used to determine the group of an element.

Example — Identifying group from IE data

Successive IEs of element X (kJ mol⁻¹): 578, 1820, 2750, 11600, 14800

1
Large jump between IE₃ and IE₄ (2750 → 11600)
2
3 electrons removed easily before the jump → element is in Group 3
2.6

Trends in First Ionisation Energy

TrendDirectionReason
Down a groupIE₁ decreasesMore electron shells → greater shielding → outer electron further from nucleus and less attracted
Across a periodIE₁ generally increasesNuclear charge increases → stronger attraction on outer electrons at similar distance
Two anomalies across Period 2

Be → B: IE₁ drops. B (2s²2p¹) removes electron from 2p — higher energy, further from nucleus, easier to remove than Be's 2s².

N → O: IE₁ drops. N has 2p³ (half-filled, extra stable). O has 2p⁴ — the paired electrons in one 2p repel each other, making the 4th 2p electron easier to remove.

QUIZ

25-Question Quiz

Questions — Click "Show" for each answer

Q1

State Bohr's model of the atom. What is a key limitation?

Electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed circular energy levels (shells). Key limitation: it only works well for hydrogen; fails for multi-electron atoms.
Q2

What causes the emission spectrum of hydrogen?

Electrons are excited to higher energy levels, then fall back to lower levels, releasing photons of specific energy (ΔE = hf). Each transition produces a specific wavelength of light.
Q3

What is an atomic orbital?

A region of space where there is a high probability (90–95%) of finding an electron. Each orbital holds a maximum of 2 electrons with opposite spins.
Q4

How many orbitals are in the 3d sub-shell? How many electrons can it hold?

5 orbitals in the 3d sub-shell, holding a maximum of 10 electrons.
Q5

State the Aufbau principle.

Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy, starting with the lowest energy orbital available.
Q6

State Hund's rule.

When filling degenerate (equal energy) orbitals, electrons occupy separate orbitals with parallel spins before pairing up. This minimises electron-electron repulsion.
Q7

Write the full electron configuration of phosphorus (Z=15).

1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p³
Q8

Write the electron configuration of Fe²⁺ (Z=26).

Fe: [Ar] 3d⁶ 4s². Fe²⁺ loses the two 4s electrons first: [Ar] 3d⁶
Q9

Why does 4s fill before 3d even though 3d is in a lower principal shell?

4s has lower energy than 3d due to the effect of electron penetration and shielding. The 4s orbital is closer to the nucleus on average for neutral atoms, so electrons occupy it first.
Q10

Write the electron configuration of Cl⁻ (Z=17).

Cl: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁵. Cl⁻ gains one electron: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ = [Ar]
Q11

Define first ionisation energy. Write the equation for the first IE of sodium.

Energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms. Na(g) → Na⁺(g) + e⁻   ΔH = IE₁
Q12

Why do successive ionisation energies always increase?

Each successive electron is removed from a more positively charged ion. The remaining electrons are more strongly attracted to the nucleus and harder to remove.
Q13

The successive IEs of an element are: 496, 4563, 6913, 9543, 13354 kJ mol⁻¹. Which group is this element in?

Large jump between IE₁ (496) and IE₂ (4563) → only 1 electron removed easily → Group 1. This is sodium.
Q14

Why does IE₁ generally increase across Period 2 from Li to Ne?

Nuclear charge increases from Li (Z=3) to Ne (Z=10) while electrons are added to the same shell (2), so shielding is similar. Increased nuclear charge pulls outer electrons more strongly, requiring more energy to remove them.
Q15

Explain why the IE₁ of boron is less than that of beryllium.

Be removes from 2s² (lower energy); B removes from 2p¹ (higher energy sub-shell, slightly further from nucleus, more shielded by 2s electrons). The 2p electron is easier to remove.
Q16

Explain why the IE₁ of oxygen is less than that of nitrogen.

N has a half-filled 2p³ configuration — extra stable (no paired electrons). O has 2p⁴ — one pair of electrons repel each other, making the 4th 2p electron easier to remove.
Q17

Write the anomalous electron configuration of copper (Z=29) and explain why it is anomalous.

[Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹. Expected: [Ar] 3d⁹ 4s². A fully filled 3d¹⁰ sub-shell is extra stable, so one electron moves from 4s to 3d.
Q18

How many electrons are in the 3rd shell of a sulphur atom (Z=16)?

S: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁴. Third shell (n=3): 3s² + 3p⁴ = 6 electrons.
Q19

State the Pauli exclusion principle.

No two electrons in an atom can have the same four quantum numbers. In practice: each orbital can hold a maximum of 2 electrons, and they must have opposite spins (↑↓).
Q20

Write the electron configuration of Cr (Z=24).

[Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ (anomalous — half-filled 3d is extra stable)
Q21

What is meant by "shielding" in atomic structure?

Inner electrons repel outer electrons and partially shield them from the full nuclear charge. The effective nuclear charge felt by an outer electron is less than the actual nuclear charge.
Q22

Why does IE₁ decrease down Group 1?

Down Group 1, each element has more electron shells, increasing shielding and distance of the outer electron from the nucleus. Despite increasing nuclear charge, the outer electron is much less attracted and easier to remove.
Q23

The Balmer series in hydrogen's emission spectrum is in the visible region. What level do electrons fall to?

n=2 (second energy level). Electrons fall from higher levels (n=3,4,5,6) down to n=2, releasing visible light.
Q24

Write the electron configuration of Mn²⁺ (Z=25).

Mn: [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s². Mn²⁺ loses both 4s electrons: [Ar] 3d⁵
Q25

How can successive ionisation energies be used to determine how many electrons an atom has in its outer shell?

A large jump in successive IE values indicates moving from the outer shell to the next inner shell. The number of electrons removed before the large jump = the number of outer shell electrons = the group number.

Explain why there is a large jump in ionisation energy between IE₂ and IE₃ for magnesium (Z=12).

[1] Mg has configuration [Ne] 3s². IE₁ and IE₂ remove the 2 electrons in 3s. [1] IE₃ requires removing an electron from the 2p sub-shell of the inner shell (n=2). [1] These electrons are much closer to the nucleus, experience much greater nuclear attraction, and are much harder to remove.

Describe the trend in first ionisation energies across Period 3 (Na to Ar). Explain two anomalies.

[1] Generally increases across the period due to increasing nuclear charge. [1] Mg→Al drop: Al removes from higher-energy 3p sub-shell, which is more shielded than Mg's 3s. [1] P→S drop: P has half-filled 3p³ (extra stable); S has 3p⁴ with a paired electron — repulsion makes it easier to remove.
A6 [3]

Explain the anomalous electron configurations of Cu and Cr.

[1] Cr: [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ — a half-filled 3d sub-shell has extra stability, so one 4s electron moves to 3d. [1] Cu: [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹ — a fully filled 3d sub-shell has extra stability, so one 4s electron moves to 3d. [1] Both cases: the stability gained by completing/half-filling the 3d sub-shell outweighs the energy cost.

Section C — Extended (10 marks)

C1 [5]

Explain how the first ionisation energies of the elements provide evidence for the existence of electron shells and sub-shells.

[1] Large jumps in successive IEs for one element show electrons in different shells — inner electrons much harder to remove. [1] Gradual increase in IE₁ across a period shows electrons being added to the same shell, with increasing nuclear charge. [1] Dips at the start of each period (e.g. Li, Na) confirm new shell begins (electron further from nucleus, more shielded). [1] Anomalies at B and O confirm sub-shells — removal from a higher-energy sub-shell (2p) is easier than from 2s. [1] The specific pattern of IE₁ values across periods repeats, confirming the periodic filling of shells and sub-shells.
C2 [5]

Compare Bohr's model of the atom with the modern orbital model. Describe what each can and cannot explain.

[1] Bohr: electrons in fixed circular orbits at set distances. Modern: electrons in orbitals (probability regions) of defined shapes (s,p,d,f). [1] Bohr successfully explains hydrogen's line spectrum (specific energy transitions). [1] Bohr fails for multi-electron atoms and cannot explain fine structure. [1] Modern model explains sub-shells, Hund's rule, anomalous configurations, and complex spectra. [1] Modern model based on wave mechanics — electron described by wavefunction; its position is probabilistic, not fixed.
