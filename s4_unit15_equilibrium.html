15.1

Reversible Reactions

What is a Reversible Reaction?

A reversible reaction is one that can proceed in both the forward and reverse directions. It is shown with a double arrow (⇌):

aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

The forward reaction converts reactants (A, B) to products (C, D). The reverse reaction converts products back to reactants. Not all reactions are reversible — irreversible reactions go essentially to completion (single arrow →).

15.2

Dynamic Equilibrium

Definition — Dynamic Equilibrium A state reached in a closed system when the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction. The concentrations of all species remain constant — but both reactions continue simultaneously at the same rate. The system appears static but is actually in constant motion at the molecular level.

Key Features of Dynamic Equilibrium

  • Closed system required — no products or reactants can escape
  • Constant macroscopic properties — concentrations, colour, pressure, and density do not change
  • Dynamic at the molecular level — forward and reverse reactions both ongoing; rates are equal (not zero)
  • Can be reached from either direction — same equilibrium state is reached whether you start with reactants or products
  • Temperature dependent — the equilibrium position changes with temperature; a new equilibrium is established
15.3

Le Châtelier's Principle

Le Châtelier's Principle (1888) "If a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change (in concentration, pressure, or temperature), the system will shift its equilibrium position to partially oppose and minimise the effect of that change."

Why "Partially Oppose"?

The system never fully neutralises the disturbance — it only partially counteracts it. For example, if you add more reactant, the equilibrium shifts forward to use some of it up — but not all. A new equilibrium is established with higher concentrations of both reactants and products than the original equilibrium, but with a higher proportion of products.

15.4

Effects of Changes on Equilibrium Position

1. Effect of Concentration

For: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

ChangeShiftEffect on concentrations
Increase [A] or [B]→ Forward (right)[C] and [D] increase; [A] and [B] partially decrease
Decrease [A] or [B]← Reverse (left)[C] and [D] decrease; [A] and [B] partially increase
Increase [C] or [D]← Reverse (left)[A] and [B] increase; [C] and [D] partially decrease
Remove product C or D→ Forward (right)More products formed — used in synthesis to improve yield
💡
Kc is NOT affected by concentration changes Adding or removing reactants/products shifts the equilibrium position but does NOT change the value of Kc. The equilibrium constant Kc only changes with temperature.

2. Effect of Pressure (for gaseous equilibria)

Pressure changes only affect equilibria involving gases. The effect depends on the total moles of gas on each side:

ChangeShiftCondition
Increase pressure→ Side with fewer moles of gasReduces the "pressure" by producing fewer gas molecules
Decrease pressure→ Side with more moles of gasIncreases gas molecules to partially restore pressure
Equal moles of gas on both sidesNo shiftPressure change has no effect on position
Worked Example 15.1 — Pressure Effect

For N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) — left side: 4 mol gas; right side: 2 mol gas. Increasing pressure → shifts right (fewer moles gas) → more NH₃ produced. Decreasing pressure → shifts left → less NH₃.

For H₂(g) + I₂(g) ⇌ 2HI(g) — left: 2 mol; right: 2 mol. Pressure change has NO effect on the equilibrium position (Kc unchanged).

💡
Adding an inert gas at constant volume — no effect on equilibrium position (partial pressures and concentrations of reactants/products unchanged). Adding inert gas at constant pressure — volume increases → partial pressures decrease → shifts toward more moles of gas side.

3. Effect of Temperature

Temperature is the only factor that changes the value of Kc (or Kp) — not just the position:

Reaction typeIncrease temperatureDecrease temperatureEffect on Kc
Exothermic (ΔH < 0): A ⇌ B + heatShifts left (← reverse) — opposes heat inputShifts right (→ forward) — generates heatIncrease T → Kc decreases (less product at equilibrium)
Endothermic (ΔH > 0): A + heat ⇌ BShifts right (→ forward) — uses up added heatShifts left (← reverse)Increase T → Kc increases (more product at equilibrium)
🧠
Temperature is the ONLY factor that changes Kc Concentration, pressure, and catalysts change the equilibrium POSITION but NOT the value of Kc. Only a change in temperature alters Kc. A catalyst speeds up both forward and reverse reactions equally → equilibrium reached faster but Kc is unchanged.

4. Effect of a Catalyst

A catalyst speeds up both the forward and reverse reactions equally by providing an alternative pathway with lower activation energy. Therefore:

  • Equilibrium is reached faster
  • The equilibrium position is unchanged (same concentrations at equilibrium)
  • Kc is unchanged
  • A catalyst does NOT improve the yield — it only saves time

Exercises

  • Define the terms: (a) reversible reaction (b) irreversible reaction (c) dynamic equilibrium. Give one example of each from everyday chemistry and write a balanced equation where possible.
    Reversible reaction: proceeds in both forward and reverse directions; can reach equilibrium. Example: N2(g) + 3H2(g) ⇌ 2NH3(g). Irreversible: proceeds essentially to completion in one direction only. Example: 2Mg + O2 → 2MgO (combustion). Dynamic equilibrium: a state in a closed system where forward rate = reverse rate and all concentrations remain constant. Example: N2O4(g) ⇌ 2NO2(g) — the brown colour stays constant in a sealed tube.
  • For the reaction PCl5(g) ⇌ PCl3(g) + Cl2(g) (ΔH = +88 kJ mol−1), predict the effect on the equilibrium position of: (a) increasing temperature (b) increasing pressure (c) adding a catalyst (d) removing Cl2 as it forms. In each case, state the direction of shift and explain using Le Châtelier's Principle.
    (a) Increase T: forward reaction is endothermic (ΔH = +88 kJ/mol) → shifts RIGHT → more PCl3 and Cl2. (b) Increase P: left = 1 mol gas; right = 1+1 = 2 mol gas → fewer moles on LEFT → shifts LEFT → more PCl5. (c) Catalyst: no change in position — equilibrium reached faster only. (d) Remove Cl2: [Cl2] decreases → system opposes by producing more Cl2 → shifts RIGHT.
  • State Le Châtelier's Principle. Apply it to N2(g) + 3H2(g) ⇌ 2NH3(g) (exothermic). Predict the effect on equilibrium position of: (a) increasing pressure from 100 to 300 atm (b) increasing temperature from 350°C to 500°C (c) removing NH3 as it forms (d) adding an iron catalyst.
    Le Châtelier's Principle: when a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to oppose the change. N2 + 3H2 ⇌ 2NH3 (exothermic): (a) Increase P from 100→300 atm: 4 mol gas → 2 mol gas → shifts RIGHT → more NH3. (b) Increase T 350→500°C: exothermic forward → shifts LEFT → less NH3; lower yield. (c) Remove NH3: [NH3] drops → shifts RIGHT continuously → more NH3 produced. (d) Iron catalyst: no effect on position; equilibrium reached faster.
  • For the equilibrium 2NO2(g) ⇌ N2O4(g) (exothermic, ΔH = −57 kJ/mol), describe and explain what you observe if: (a) the sealed tube is placed in hot water (b) the sealed tube is placed in ice water (c) the pressure is increased by halving the volume. (NO2 is brown; N2O4 is colourless.)
    (a) Hot water: adds heat → system opposes by absorbing heat → endothermic direction favoured → more N2O4 → 2NO2 → mixture darkens (more brown NO2). (b) Ice water: removes heat → exothermic direction favoured → 2NO2 → N2O4 → mixture pales (more colourless N2O4). (c) Halve volume (double P): left = 2 mol gas; right = 1 mol gas; fewer moles on right → shifts RIGHT → more N2O4 formed → mixture pales initially then stabilises.
  • Explain the industrial conditions for the Haber Process (N2 + 3H2 ⇌ 2NH3, exothermic): (a) Why is ~450°C chosen rather than a very low temperature? (b) Why is ~200 atm pressure used? (c) What is the role of the iron catalyst? (d) Why is ammonia removed by condensation as it forms?
    (a) ~450°C chosen: low T gives better yield (exothermic → shifts right at low T) but rate is too slow; 450°C is a compromise giving acceptable rate AND yield with the iron catalyst. (b) ~200 atm: 4 mol gas → 2 mol; high P shifts right → better yield; limited by engineering cost and safety. (c) Iron catalyst: lowers activation energy for both forward and reverse equally; allows acceptable rate at 450°C without wasting energy on extreme temperatures; does NOT affect yield. (d) NH3 removed by condensation: continuously lowers [NH3] → Le Châtelier shifts right → unreacted N2/H2 recycled → near-complete conversion over multiple passes.
  • For H2(g) + I2(g) ⇌ 2HI(g): (a) Explain why changing the pressure has no effect on the equilibrium position. (b) A student claims that adding more H2 shifts the equilibrium right. Explain this at the molecular level. (c) Why does the same equilibrium mixture result whether you start from pure H2 + I2 or from pure HI?
    (a) H2 + I2 ⇌ 2HI: left = 1+1 = 2 mol gas; right = 2 mol gas. Equal moles → Δn(gas) = 0 → pressure change cannot favour either side → no shift. (b) Adding H2 increases [H2] → forward rate increases (more H2-I2 collisions) → more HI produced until new equilibrium where forward rate = reverse rate again. (c) Equilibrium is a state function determined only by T and the amounts present. Starting from either H2+I2 or pure HI with equivalent amounts, the system shifts forward or backward respectively until it reaches the same ratio of concentrations (same Kc) at that temperature.

Multiple Choice Quiz — 25 Questions

Unit 15: Chemical Equilibrium

25 Questions
Q1

What distinguishes a reversible reaction from an irreversible reaction?

A reversible reaction can go in both directions: reactants → products AND products → reactants. An irreversible reaction proceeds essentially to completion in one direction only.
Q2

At dynamic equilibrium, which statement is CORRECT?

At dynamic equilibrium: (1) rates of forward and reverse reactions are equal; (2) concentrations remain constant; (3) the reaction is still occurring at molecular level. The system appears static macroscopically but is dynamic microscopically.
Q3

Le Châtelier's Principle states that when a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it will:

Le Châtelier's Principle: if a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change (concentration, pressure, or temperature), the equilibrium position will shift in a direction that opposes the change and establishes a new equilibrium.
Q4

For 2SO2(g) + O2(g) ⇌ 2SO3(g) (exothermic), increasing pressure will shift the equilibrium:

Left side: 2 + 1 = 3 moles of gas. Right side: 2 moles of gas. Increasing pressure favours the side with fewer moles of gas → shifts RIGHT → more SO3. Le Châtelier: system opposes increased pressure by reducing the number of gas molecules.
Q5

For the exothermic reaction A(g) ⇌ B(g) + heat, increasing temperature will:

Heat can be treated as a product in an exothermic reaction. Adding more heat (increasing temperature) shifts the equilibrium LEFT (away from the heat side) to oppose the change. This decreases the yield of products.
Q6

Adding a catalyst to a reaction at equilibrium:

A catalyst lowers the activation energy for BOTH forward and reverse reactions equally. It does NOT shift the equilibrium position, does NOT change the yield, and does NOT change the equilibrium constant. It only allows equilibrium to be reached FASTER.
Q7

Which change will shift the equilibrium RIGHT for: CaCO3(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO2(g)?

Removing CO2 decreases [CO2] → equilibrium shifts right to replace it (Le Châtelier). Adding more CaCO3 (a pure solid) has no effect on position. Increasing pressure shifts LEFT (fewer gas moles). Decreasing temperature shifts LEFT for this endothermic forward reaction.
Q8

For H2(g) + I2(g) ⇌ 2HI(g), increasing pressure has:

Moles of gas on left = 1 + 1 = 2. Moles on right = 2. Equal moles on both sides → pressure change has NO effect on the equilibrium position. Le Châtelier: there is no way to oppose a pressure change if both sides have the same number of moles.
Q9

For the reaction: Fe3+(aq) + SCN−(aq) ⇌ [FeSCN]2+(aq) [blood red], adding more Fe(NO3)3 will:

Adding Fe3+ increases [Fe3+] → equilibrium shifts RIGHT to reduce [Fe3+] → more [FeSCN]2+ is formed → the solution becomes a deeper blood-red colour. This is a classic demonstration of Le Châtelier's Principle using colour change as evidence.
Q10

Which of the following is an example of a REVERSIBLE reaction?

N2O4(g) ⇌ 2NO2(g) is reversible — both decomposition and recombination occur, achieving equilibrium. The other three are highly exothermic irreversible reactions that proceed essentially to completion; the products do not recombine under normal conditions.
Q11

For the endothermic reaction: N2(g) + O2(g) ⇌ 2NO(g), maximum yield of NO is favoured by:

The forward reaction is endothermic, so increasing temperature shifts the equilibrium RIGHT → more NO. Since moles of gas are equal on both sides (2 vs 2), pressure has no effect on position.
Q12

At equilibrium in a closed system, which of the following is NOT true?

Concentrations are CONSTANT at equilibrium, but they are NOT necessarily equal to each other. For example, in H2 + I2 ⇌ 2HI, [HI] is typically much higher than [H2] or [I2].
Q13

For: CH3COOH + C2H5OH ⇌ CH3COOC2H5 + H2O, which change increases the yield of ester?

Removing ester (product) as it forms shifts the equilibrium RIGHT (Le Châtelier), producing more ester. Adding water (a product) shifts LEFT. Temperature and container size have little practical effect on this liquid-phase equilibrium.
Q14

For the Haber Process: N2(g) + 3H2(g) ⇌ 2NH3(g) (exothermic), which conditions give the BEST yield of NH3?

High pressure: 4 moles gas → 2 moles → shifts RIGHT (more NH3). Low temperature: forward reaction is exothermic → lower T shifts RIGHT (more NH3). (In practice, 450°C is a compromise to get adequate rate.)
Q15

When more reactant is added to a system at equilibrium, the forward reaction rate initially:

Adding reactant increases its concentration → collision frequency increases → forward rate increases. The equilibrium shifts right until a new balance is achieved where forward rate = reverse rate again.
Q16

A closed container holds H2(g) + I2(g) ⇌ 2HI(g) at equilibrium. The volume is suddenly doubled. The equilibrium position:

Moles of gas: left = 2, right = 2. Doubling the volume (halving the pressure) has NO effect on this equilibrium because both sides have equal moles of gas. Le Châtelier: no net shift can oppose a pressure change when Δn(gas) = 0.
Q17

PCl5(g) ⇌ PCl3(g) + Cl2(g) is endothermic. Decreasing the temperature will:

Forward reaction is endothermic. Removing heat (decreasing T) shifts the equilibrium LEFT (towards the exothermic direction) to generate heat. This produces more PCl5 and decreases the amounts of PCl3 and Cl2.
Q18

In a reversible reaction, when a product is continuously removed from the equilibrium mixture:

Continuously removing a product keeps its concentration low → equilibrium keeps shifting right to replace it. This is used industrially (e.g. removing NH3 by condensation in Haber Process) to achieve near-complete conversion.
Q19

Why must a reversible reaction be carried out in a CLOSED system to achieve equilibrium?

In an open system, products escape → reverse reaction cannot occur → no equilibrium is reached. In a closed system, both reactants and products are retained, allowing forward and reverse rates to become equal.
Q20

For 2SO2(g) + O2(g) ⇌ 2SO3(g), which change does NOT alter the equilibrium POSITION?

A catalyst speeds up both forward and reverse reactions equally → equilibrium is reached faster but the position (and yield) is unchanged. Adding O2, changing pressure, or changing temperature all shift the position.
Q21

A sealed syringe with N2O4(g) ⇌ 2NO2(g) at equilibrium (N2O4 colourless; NO2 brown) is rapidly compressed. What is observed immediately, then after a few seconds?

Compression increases total pressure → all concentrations increase → immediate darkening (more NO2 per unit volume). Then Le Châtelier shifts equilibrium LEFT (fewer moles) → some NO2 recombines → slight paling from the darkest point, but still darker than original.
Q22

Why is a catalyst used in industrial reversible reactions if it does not increase yield?

Lower temperature gives better yield for exothermic reactions but is too slow. A catalyst at lower temperature can give acceptable rate with better yield — the economic optimum. Without the catalyst, the reaction would need a much higher (yield-reducing) temperature.
Q23

The reaction H2(g) + Cl2(g) → 2HCl(g) is considered irreversible under normal conditions because:

Highly exothermic reactions that produce very stable products have such large equilibrium constants that virtually all reactants are converted to products. The reverse reaction, though possible in principle, is negligible under normal conditions.
Q24

For an exothermic equilibrium reaction, increasing temperature shifts the equilibrium to the LEFT because:

Le Châtelier: adding heat → system opposes by absorbing heat → endothermic direction is favoured → left shift (for exothermic forward reaction). The equilibrium constant Kc DECREASES at higher temperature for exothermic reactions.
Q25

In the Contact Process, SO3 is absorbed into H2SO4 as soon as it forms: 2SO2 + O2 ⇌ 2SO3. This is done to:

Removing SO3 (product) keeps [SO3] very low → equilibrium shifts right (Le Châtelier) → more SO2 and O2 are consumed → near-complete conversion achieved. This is an industrial application of Le Châtelier's Principle.

Q4 [5 marks]

Distinguish between a reversible and an irreversible reaction with two examples of each. Explain why reversible reactions must be carried out in a closed system to reach equilibrium. Why does starting from either pure reactants or pure products (in the same amounts) give the same equilibrium mixture?

Reversible: reaction can go in both directions, reaching equilibrium; examples: N2O4 ⇌ 2NO2; N2 + 3H2 ⇌ 2NH3. [1] Irreversible: proceeds essentially to completion in one direction; examples: Mg + O2 → MgO (combustion); Na + H2O → NaOH + H2. [2] (c) Catalyst lowers activation energy for BOTH forward and reverse reactions equally → both rates increase by the same factor → the ratio of rates (and hence equilibrium position) is unchanged; only time to reach equilibrium decreases. Q6 [5 marks]

Sealed tubes containing N2O4(g)/NO2(g) at equilibrium are placed in (a) hot water and (b) ice. The hot tube becomes darker brown; the cold tube becomes paler. Explain these observations fully using Le Châtelier's Principle, and state what they reveal about the enthalpy of the forward reaction.

(a) Hot water: adding heat to the equilibrium → system opposes by absorbing heat → shifts in the endothermic direction → more N2O4 breaks down to give more brown NO2 → mixture darkens. [2] (b) Ice: removing heat → system opposes by releasing heat → shifts in the exothermic direction → more NO2 recombines to N2O4 (colourless) → mixture pales.
