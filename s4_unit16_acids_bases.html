16.1

Acid-Base Theories

Arrhenius Theory (1884)

The earliest theory, applicable only to aqueous solutions:

Arrhenius Acid A substance that dissociates in water to produce H⁺ ions (protons). e.g. HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻; H₂SO₄ → 2H⁺ + SO₄²⁻
Arrhenius Base A substance that dissociates in water to produce OH⁻ ions. e.g. NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻; Ca(OH)₂ → Ca²⁺ + 2OH⁻

Limitations: Cannot explain why NH₃ is a base (no OH⁻ in its formula), or acid-base reactions in non-aqueous solvents.

Brønsted-Lowry Theory (1923)

A broader theory based on proton (H⁺) transfer:

Brønsted-Lowry Acid A proton (H⁺) donor — loses a proton to another species.
Brønsted-Lowry Base A proton (H⁺) acceptor — gains a proton from an acid.

In every Brønsted-Lowry acid-base reaction, a proton is transferred. There is always a conjugate acid-base pair:

HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻ acid₁ + base₂ → acid₂ + base₁ (HCl/Cl⁻ is conjugate pair 1; H₃O⁺/H₂O is conjugate pair 2) NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻ base₁ + acid₂ → acid₁ + base₂ (NH₄⁺/NH₃ is conjugate pair 1; H₂O/OH⁻ is conjugate pair 2)
💡
Conjugate acid-base pairs differ by ONE proton (H⁺) HCl and Cl⁻ are a conjugate pair (differ by H⁺). NH₄⁺ and NH₃ are a conjugate pair. H₂O can act as either acid (donates H⁺ → OH⁻) or base (accepts H⁺ → H₃O⁺) — water is amphiprotic.

Lewis Theory (1923)

The most general theory — based on electron pair donation:

Lewis Acid An electron pair acceptor — accepts a lone pair of electrons to form a coordinate bond.
Lewis Base An electron pair donor — donates a lone pair to a Lewis acid.
BF₃ + NH₃ → F₃B←NH₃ (BF₃ = Lewis acid, has empty p orbital; NH₃ = Lewis base) AlCl₃ + Cl⁻ → AlCl₄⁻ (AlCl₃ = Lewis acid; Cl⁻ = Lewis base) Fe³⁺ + 6H₂O → [Fe(H₂O)₆]³⁺ (Fe³⁺ = Lewis acid; H₂O = Lewis base) Cu²⁺ + 4NH₃ → [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺ (Cu²⁺ = Lewis acid; NH₃ = Lewis base)

Lewis theory explains all Brønsted-Lowry reactions (H⁺ is a Lewis acid — accepts lone pair from a base) and extends to reactions without H⁺ transfer, including complex ion formation and many organic reactions.

Exercises

  • State the Arrhenius definitions of an acid and a base. Give two limitations of the Arrhenius theory and explain how the Brønsted-Lowry theory overcomes them.
    Arrhenius acid: produces H+ (H3O+) in water. Arrhenius base: produces OH− in water. Limitations: (1) only applies to aqueous solutions; (2) cannot explain basic behaviour of NH3 (no OH− group). Brønsted-Lowry overcomes these: (1) applies to non-aqueous and gas-phase reactions; (2) defines acid/base by H+ transfer — NH3 is a base because it accepts H+, regardless of water.
  • For each of the following reactions, identify: (i) the Brønsted-Lowry acid (ii) the Brønsted-Lowry base (iii) conjugate acid (iv) conjugate base: (a) HCl + H2O → H3O+ + Cl− (b) NH3 + H2O ⇌ NH4+ + OH− (c) HCO3− + OH− → CO32− + H2O (d) HSO4− + H2O ⇌ SO42− + H3O+
    (a) HCl + H2O → H3O+ + Cl−: HCl = acid; H2O = base; H3O+ = conjugate acid; Cl− = conjugate base. (b) NH3 + H2O ⇌ NH4+ + OH−: H2O = acid; NH3 = base; NH4+ = conjugate acid; OH− = conjugate base. (c) HCO3− + OH− → CO32− + H2O: HCO3− = acid; OH− = base; H2O = conjugate acid; CO32− = conjugate base. (d) HSO4− + H2O ⇌ SO42− + H3O+: HSO4− = acid; H2O = base; H3O+ = conjugate acid; SO42− = conjugate base.
  • Explain the difference between a strong acid and a weak acid, with two examples of each. Describe one experimental observation that distinguishes a strong acid from a weak acid at the same concentration.
    Strong acid: fully (100%) dissociated in dilute solution. Examples: HCl, HNO3, H2SO4, HBr. Weak acid: only partially dissociated (<5%). Examples: CH3COOH, HF, H2CO3, HCN. Experimental distinction at same concentration: (1) pH — strong acid has lower pH (e.g. HCl 0.1 mol dm−3 has pH 1; CH3COOH has pH ~2.9); (2) Reaction rate with Mg or CaCO3 — strong acid reacts more vigorously (more H+ available at same concentration).
  • State the Lewis definitions of an acid and a base. For each, identify the Lewis acid and Lewis base: (a) BF3 + :NH3 → F3B–NH3 (b) Fe3+ + 6H2O → [Fe(H2O)6]3+ (c) AlCl3 + Cl− → AlCl4− (d) CO2 + H2O → H2CO3
    Lewis acid: electron pair acceptor. Lewis base: electron pair donor. (a) BF3 + :NH3 → F3B–NH3: BF3 = Lewis acid (empty p orbital on B, accepts lone pair); NH3 = Lewis base (lone pair on N). (b) Fe3+ + 6H2O → [Fe(H2O)6]3+: Fe3+ = Lewis acid (empty d orbitals); H2O = Lewis base (lone pairs on O). (c) AlCl3 + Cl− → AlCl4−: AlCl3 = Lewis acid (incomplete octet on Al); Cl− = Lewis base. (d) CO2 + H2O → H2CO3: CO2 = Lewis acid (accepts lone pair from O in H2O); H2O = Lewis base.
  • Explain why H2O, HCO3−, and HSO4− are all described as amphiprotic. Write equations showing each species acting as both an acid and a base.
    H2O as acid: H2O → OH− + H+ (donates H+ to NH3: H2O + NH2− → OH− + NH3). H2O as base: H2O + H+ → H3O+ (from HCl). HCO3− as acid: HCO3− → CO32− + H+. HCO3− as base: HCO3− + H+ → H2CO3. HSO4− as acid: HSO4− → SO42− + H+. HSO4− as base: HSO4− + H+ → H2SO4.
  • Compare the Arrhenius, Brønsted-Lowry, and Lewis theories of acids and bases in terms of: (a) definition of an acid (b) definition of a base (c) scope (which reactions each explains) (d) one example unique to each broader theory.
    Arrhenius: acid = H+ producer in H2O; base = OH− producer in H2O; limited to aqueous. Brønsted-Lowry: acid = H+ donor; base = H+ acceptor; applies to non-aqueous/gas-phase. Lewis: acid = electron pair acceptor; base = electron pair donor; most general, covers reactions with no H+. Unique to B-L (not Arrhenius): HCl(g) + NH3(g) → NH4Cl(s) — gas phase, no water. Unique to Lewis (not B-L): BF3 + F− → BF4− — no H+ transfer at all.

Multiple Choice Quiz — 25 Questions

Unit 16: Acids and Bases

25 Questions
Q1

According to Brønsted-Lowry theory, a BASE is:

Brønsted-Lowry base = proton (H+) acceptor. Brønsted-Lowry acid = proton donor. The Brønsted-Lowry theory is broader than Arrhenius: bases do not need to contain OH− or be dissolved in water.
Q2

What is the conjugate base of H2SO4?

H2SO4 donates one proton → HSO4− is the conjugate base. Conjugate base = acid − one H+. HSO4− can itself donate a second proton, making SO42− its conjugate base.
Q3

In the reaction NH3 + H2O ⇌ NH4+ + OH−, water acts as:

H2O donates a proton (H+) to NH3 → H2O is the Brønsted-Lowry acid. NH3 accepts the proton → NH3 is the Brønsted-Lowry base. Conjugate pairs: NH3/NH4+ and H2O/OH−.
Q4

Which of the following is a WEAK acid?

Weak acids are only partially dissociated in solution. CH3COOH ⇌ CH3COO− + H+ (only ~1% dissociated at 0.1 M). HCl, HNO3, and H2SO4 are strong acids — essentially fully dissociated.
Q5

In the Arrhenius theory, an ACID is defined as:

Arrhenius acid: produces H+ (or H3O+) ions when dissolved in water. Arrhenius base: produces OH− ions in water. This is more limited than Brønsted-Lowry, as it applies only to aqueous solutions.
Q6

What is the conjugate acid of NH3?

Conjugate acid = base + one H+. NH3 + H+ → NH4+. So NH4+ is the conjugate acid of NH3. Conversely, NH3 is the conjugate base of NH4+.
Q7

HCO3− (hydrogen carbonate ion) is described as AMPHIPROTIC because:

Amphiprotic species can donate OR accept a proton. HCO3− + H+ → H2CO3 (acts as base); HCO3− → CO32− + H+ (acts as acid). Other amphiprotic species: H2O, HSO4−, H2PO4−.
Q8

In the reaction: BF3 + :NH3 → F3B←NH3, which species is the Lewis ACID?

Lewis acid = electron pair acceptor. BF3 has an empty p orbital on B → accepts the lone pair from NH3. Lewis base = electron pair donor. NH3 has a lone pair → donates it to BF3.
Q9

Which of the following is a Lewis ACID?

Lewis acid = electron pair acceptor. BF3 has an incomplete octet on boron → can accept a lone pair. NH3, H2O, and Cl− all have lone pairs → they are Lewis bases.
Q10

In the Brønsted-Lowry theory, identify the TWO conjugate pairs in: HCl + H2O → H3O+ + Cl−

Conjugate pair 1: HCl (acid) and Cl− (conjugate base) — HCl loses H+. Conjugate pair 2: H2O (base) and H3O+ (conjugate acid) — H2O gains H+. A conjugate pair differs by exactly one H+.
Q11

Which statement correctly compares Arrhenius and Brønsted-Lowry theories?

Brønsted-Lowry extends Arrhenius: (1) applies to non-aqueous solvents; (2) covers gas-phase reactions (e.g. HCl(g) + NH3(g) → NH4Cl(s)); (3) defines acids/bases by H+ transfer rather than requiring water.
Q12

In the reaction: CH3COOH + H2O ⇌ CH3COO− + H3O+, the conjugate base of CH3COOH is:

CH3COOH donates H+ to H2O → CH3COO− is the conjugate base. The conjugate base of an acid always has one fewer H+ and one more negative charge.
Q13

H2O is amphoteric because:

H2O as acid: H2O → OH− + H+ (in reaction with stronger base like NH3). H2O as base: H2O + H+ → H3O+ (in reaction with HCl). Amphoteric = can act as both acid and base (not necessarily amphiprotic, but for water these terms overlap).
Q14

According to Lewis theory, which species acts as a Lewis BASE in the formation of [Fe(H2O)6]3+?

H2O has lone pairs on oxygen → donates lone pairs to Fe3+ → Lewis base. Fe3+ has empty d orbitals → accepts lone pairs → Lewis acid. Lewis theory explains complex ion formation that Brønsted-Lowry cannot.
Q15

A strong acid is one that:

Strong acid: fully dissociated in solution (e.g. HCl → H+ + Cl−, 100%). Weak acid: partially dissociated (e.g. CH3COOH ⇌ CH3COO− + H+, ~1%). Strength is independent of concentration.
Q16

Which of the following pairs are a conjugate acid-base pair?

A conjugate acid-base pair differs by exactly one H+. NH4+ → NH3 + H+ ✓. H2SO4 and SO42− differ by two H+ → not a conjugate pair. HCl and NaOH are not related by H+ transfer.
Q17

In the gas phase reaction: HCl(g) + NH3(g) → NH4Cl(s), which acid-base theory BEST explains this?

No water is present → Arrhenius cannot explain it. HCl donates H+ to NH3 → Brønsted-Lowry (proton transfer) explains it perfectly. Lewis theory also works but Brønsted-Lowry is more specific here.
Q18

The strength of an acid depends on:

Acid strength = tendency to donate H+. Strong acids: bond to H+ is easily broken → 100% dissociation. Weak acids: H+ is held more tightly → partial dissociation. Strength is an intrinsic property of the acid, NOT its concentration.
Q19

Which of the following correctly identifies a Lewis acid-base pair?

AlCl3 has an empty orbital → accepts lone pair from Cl− → Lewis acid. Cl− donates lone pair → Lewis base. This gives AlCl4−. Option D has acid and base reversed (BF3 is Lewis acid; NH3 is Lewis base).
Q20

What is the conjugate BASE of H3PO4?

H3PO4 loses one H+ → H2PO4− (dihydrogen phosphate). This is the conjugate base after the first deprotonation. H3PO4 is a triprotic acid with three successive conjugate pairs.
Q21

In the Brønsted-Lowry sense, a species that can act as BOTH an acid and a base is called:

Amphoteric (or amphiprotic) species: can both donate and accept a proton. Examples: H2O, HCO3−, HSO4−, H2PO4−, amino acids (have both NH3+ and COO− groups).
Q22

The Lewis theory of acids and bases is MORE general than Brønsted-Lowry because:

Lewis theory covers any electron pair transfer, including complex ion formation and reactions with no H+ involved (e.g. BF3 + F− → BF4−). Every Brønsted-Lowry acid is also a Lewis acid (accepts electron pair from water when H+ is transferred), but Lewis acids include species that cannot donate/accept H+.
Q23

In the reaction: CO2 + H2O → H2CO3, CO2 acts as a:

CO2 accepts a lone pair from H2O to form H2CO3 → Lewis acid. This cannot be explained by Brønsted-Lowry (no H+ is transferred in this step). Lewis acids include many molecules with incomplete octets or empty d orbitals.
Q24

A student dissolves equal moles of HCl and CH3COOH in separate beakers of water. Which statement is correct?

HCl is a strong acid → completely dissociated → higher [H+] → lower pH. CH3COOH is weak → partially dissociated → lower [H+] → higher pH (less acidic). At 0.1 mol/dm3: HCl has pH 1; CH3COOH has pH ~2.9.
Q25

Which of these reactions can ONLY be explained by Lewis theory and NOT by Brønsted-Lowry?

BF3 + F− → BF4− involves no H+ transfer → Brønsted-Lowry cannot explain it. BF3 is a Lewis acid (accepts lone pair); F− is a Lewis base (donates lone pair). All other options involve H+ transfer and are explained by Brønsted-Lowry.

Q1 [5 marks]

Compare the Arrhenius, Brønsted-Lowry, and Lewis definitions of acids and bases. Give one example of a reaction that is explained by Lewis theory but CANNOT be explained by the Arrhenius theory. [5]

Arrhenius: acid produces H+ in water; base produces OH− in water. Limited to aqueous solutions. [1] Brønsted-Lowry: acid = H+ donor; base = H+ acceptor. Applies to non-aqueous and gas-phase reactions. Introduces concept of conjugate pairs. [1] Lewis: acid = electron pair acceptor; base = electron pair donor. Most general — covers reactions without any H+ transfer. [1] Lewis-only example: BF3 + :NH3 → F3B←NH3. No H+ transferred → cannot be explained by Arrhenius (no OH− or H+ produced in water) or Brønsted-Lowry. BF3 accepts lone pair from NH3 → Lewis acid. [2]
Q2 [5 marks]

Identify the Brønsted-Lowry acid, base, conjugate acid, and conjugate base in each reaction: (a) H2SO4 + H2O → H3O+ + HSO4− [1] (b) HCO3− + H2O ⇌ H2CO3 + OH− [1] (c) NH4+ + OH− → NH3 + H2O [1] (d) Explain why HCO3− is described as amphiprotic, writing two equations to illustrate. [2]

(a) H2SO4 = acid (H+ donor); H2O = base; H3O+ = conjugate acid of H2O; HSO4− = conjugate base of H2SO4. [1] (b) HCO3− = base (accepts H+ from H2O); H2O = acid; H2CO3 = conjugate acid of HCO3−; OH− = conjugate base of H2O. [1] (c) NH4+ = acid (donates H+); OH− = base; H2O = conjugate acid of OH−; NH3 = conjugate base of NH4+. [1] (d) HCO3− as acid: HCO3− → CO32− + H+ (donates proton to a base). HCO3− as base: HCO3− + H+ → H2CO3 (accepts proton from an acid). Amphiprotic = can both donate and accept H+. [2]
Q3 [5 marks]

Explain the difference between a STRONG acid and a WEAK acid. For each pair, state which is the stronger acid and why: (a) HCl vs CH3COOH (b) HNO3 vs HNO2 (c) H2SO4 vs H3PO4. Describe TWO experimental observations that distinguish 0.1 mol dm−3 HCl from 0.1 mol dm−3 CH3COOH. [5]

Strong acid: fully (100%) dissociated in dilute aqueous solution. Weak acid: only partially dissociated (< 5% typically). [1] (a) HCl: strong acid (fully dissociated); CH3COOH: weak (Ka = 1.7×10−5, ~1% dissociated). HCl is stronger. [1] (b) HNO3: strong acid; HNO2: weak acid. HNO3 is stronger. [1] (c) H2SO4: strong (1st dissociation complete); H3PO4: weak acid. H2SO4 is stronger. [1] Two observations: (1) HCl has lower pH (pH 1 vs ~2.9) — more H+ ions present; (2) HCl reacts faster with Mg ribbon (or CaCO3) and produces more vigorous effervescence — more H+ available. Both from same concentration. [1]
Q4 [5 marks]

State the Lewis definitions of acid and base. For each reaction, identify the Lewis acid and Lewis base, and write an equation: (a) Formation of [Fe(H2O)6]3+ from Fe3+ and H2O (b) Reaction of SO3 with H2O to give H2SO4 (c) AlCl3 reacting with Cl− (d) Reaction of Ag+ with two NH3 molecules. [5]

Lewis acid: electron pair acceptor. Lewis base: electron pair donor. [1] (a) Fe3+ + 6H2O → [Fe(H2O)6]3+. Fe3+ = Lewis acid (empty d orbitals, accepts 6 lone pairs); H2O = Lewis base (lone pairs on O). [1] (b) SO3 + H2O → H2SO4. SO3 = Lewis acid (accepts lone pair from O in H2O); H2O = Lewis base. [1] (c) AlCl3 + Cl− → AlCl4−. AlCl3 = Lewis acid (incomplete octet on Al); Cl− = Lewis base (lone pair donor). [1] (d) Ag+ + 2NH3 → [Ag(NH3)2]+. Ag+ = Lewis acid; NH3 = Lewis base (N lone pair). [1]
Q5 [5 marks]

For the gas-phase reaction: HCl(g) + NH3(g) → NH4Cl(s), explain why this reaction: (a) CAN be explained by Brønsted-Lowry theory [2] (b) CANNOT be explained by Arrhenius theory [1] (c) Identify the conjugate acid-base pairs [2].

(a) Brønsted-Lowry: HCl donates H+ to NH3 → H+ transfer occurs. HCl = acid (H+ donor); NH3 = base (H+ acceptor). Brønsted-Lowry applies to gas-phase and non-aqueous reactions — it only requires H+ transfer, not water. [2] (b) Arrhenius: requires dissolution in water to produce H+ or OH− ions. This is a gas-phase reaction with no water. No H+ ions produced in solution; NH4Cl is a solid product. Arrhenius theory cannot describe it. [1] (c) Conjugate pair 1: HCl (acid) and Cl− (conjugate base) — HCl loses H+. Conjugate pair 2: NH3 (base) and NH4+ (conjugate acid) — NH3 gains H+. [2]
Q6 [5 marks]

Explain, with examples, the concept of a conjugate acid-base pair. For each species, write the formula of its conjugate acid OR conjugate base as indicated: (a) conjugate base of H3PO4 (b) conjugate acid of CO32− (c) conjugate base of H2O (d) conjugate acid of HPO42− (e) conjugate base of HF. [5]

Conjugate acid-base pair: two species that differ by exactly one H+. When an acid donates H+, it becomes its conjugate base (one fewer H+, one more negative charge). When a base accepts H+, it becomes its conjugate acid. [1] (a) H3PO4 − H+ = H2PO4− [1] (b) CO32− + H+ = HCO3− [1] (c) H2O − H+ = OH− [1] (d) HPO42− + H+ = H2PO4− [1] (e) HF − H+ = F− [0 — included in subtotal]
Q7 [10 marks]

(a) Explain fully, with equations, what is meant by the terms: (i) amphoteric (ii) amphiprotic. Illustrate with examples from H2O, HCO3−, and HSO4−. State whether amphoteric and amphiprotic mean exactly the same thing. [5] (b) Compare the three acid-base theories in a table with columns: Theory | Definition of Acid | Definition of Base | Limitation. Then describe one reaction that each broader theory can explain but the narrower cannot. [5]

(a) Amphoteric: can act as both acid and base (general term, includes Lewis sense). Amphiprotic: specifically can both donate AND accept a proton (H+). These terms largely overlap for Brønsted-Lowry discussion but amphoteric is broader (includes Lewis). [1] H2O as acid: H2O → OH− + H+ (with strong base NH3: H2O + NH2− → OH− + NH3). H2O as base: H2O + H+ → H3O+ (with HCl). [1] HCO3− as acid: HCO3− → CO32− + H+. As base: HCO3− + H+ → H2CO3. [1] HSO4− as acid: HSO4− → SO42− + H+. As base: HSO4− + H+ → H2SO4. [1] Difference: amphoteric covers Lewis behaviour; amphiprotic is specifically proton transfer. Strictly not identical. [1] (b) Arrhenius: acid = H+ in H2O; base = OH− in H2O; limitation = only aqueous. Brønsted-Lowry: acid = H+ donor; base = H+ acceptor; limitation = requires H+ transfer. Lewis: acid = e− pair acceptor; base = e− pair donor; limitation = broadest, least specific. [2] B-L explains what Arrhenius cannot: HCl(g) + NH3(g) → NH4Cl (gas phase, no water). Lewis explains what B-L cannot: BF3 + F− → BF4− (no H+ transfer). [3]
Q8 [10 marks]

(a) For each of the following, classify as strong acid, weak acid, strong base, or weak base and explain your reasoning: HNO3, CH3COOH, Ca(OH)2, NH3, H2SO4, HF, KOH, HCN. [4] (b) Using Brønsted-Lowry theory, write equations showing the stepwise dissociation of H3PO4. Label each species in the first step as acid, base, conjugate acid, or conjugate base. [3] (c) Explain why water is described as a neutral solvent in Brønsted-Lowry theory, and why some solvents are called levelling solvents for strong acids. [3]

(a) HNO3: strong acid (fully dissociated). CH3COOH: weak acid (Ka = 1.7×10−5, partial dissociation). Ca(OH)2: strong base (fully dissociates to Ca2+ + 2OH−). NH3: weak base (NH3 + H2O ⇌ NH4+ + OH−, Kb = 1.8×10−5). H2SO4: strong acid (1st dissociation complete). HF: weak acid (Ka = 6.3×10−4, partial). KOH: strong base. HCN: weak acid (Ka = 4.9×10−10). [4] (b) Step 1: H3PO4 (acid) + H2O (base) ⇌ H2PO4− (conjugate base) + H3O+ (conjugate acid). Step 2: H2PO4− + H2O ⇌ HPO42− + H3O+. Step 3: HPO42− + H2O ⇌ PO43− + H3O+. [3] (c) Water is neutral: it can donate H+ (to stronger bases) or accept H+ (from stronger acids) equally readily → does not favour one direction. Levelling effect: in water, all strong acids appear equally strong because they are all 100% dissociated — water accepts their H+ completely. To distinguish acid strengths among strong acids requires a weaker base solvent. [3]
